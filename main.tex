\begin{abstract} \bfseries \boldmath

% Start with one or two sentences of background
% Then summarise the results of your observations, experiments, simulations etc.
% End with a statement of your main conclusions

Increased concentrations of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere due to human activities trap heat and cause the planet to warm, leading to changes in the global climate. Despite advances in marine vessel tracking technology and emission inventories, a substantial and unquantified fraction of vessel activity remains invisible in current global datasets.  As a result, global inventories chronically underestimate marine vessel emissions and misrepresent trends, undermining efforts to monitor, regulate, and mitigate their climate impacts. We developed a method for quantifying marine vessel emissions at global scale by fusing two independent types of satellite data: vessel tracking using the Automatic Identification System (AIS) to estimate vessel-level emissions, and vessel detection using synthetic aperture radar (SAR) imagery to estimate emissions for vessels that do not use AIS. We found that vessels emitted approximately 1.3 billion metric tonnes of CO$_{2}$ in 2023, accounting for 15\% of global fossil fuel emissions from the transportation sector. We also found that marine vessel CO$_{2}$ emissions increased by 22\% between 2016 and 2023, more than five times the increase in land-based transportation emissions. Because many vessels do not broadcast AIS, current approaches solely based on AIS \textit{underestimate} the magnitude of 2024 emissions by 15\%. Meanwhile, because AIS coverage has generally been increasing over time, current global estimates \textit{overestimate} the increasing emissions trend by 54\% (from 2016 to 2024). Our approach provides a robust estimate of the magnitude, trends, and spatial patterns of marine vessel GHG emissions and non-GHG air pollutant emissions. This information can serve as a key input to designing and evaluating interventions aimed at mitigating climate change.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The transportation sector is a major source of global greenhouse gas (GHG) and local air pollution emissions, responsible for 21\% of all CO$_{2}$ emissions globally in 2023 \cite{JRC138862}. While estimation of land-based transportation emissions is well understood, fundamental gaps remain in the estimation of marine vessel emissions. These emissions come primarily from the transportation of goods via shipping vessels, carriage of passengers, and fishing activity, with a smaller contribution from other industrial activities such as dredging and offshore infrastructure maintenance. Because these diverse vessel types vary dramatically in the extent to which vessel behavior is tracked and vessel characteristics are understood, we lack a comprehensive estimate of emissions for these diverse marine activities. This knowledge gap limits both our basic understanding of marine vessel emissions, and the data granularity that is needed to underpin policy or private sector interventions aimed at reducing emissions.

The currently accepted state-of-the-art method for estimating marine vessel emissions relies on a bottom-up model that uses vessel tracking data from the Automatic Identification System (AIS), which combines information on the vessel movement  with characteristics of each vessel including main and auxiliary engine powers, boiler power, as well as pollutant-specific emission factors that specify the kg of emissions per metric tonne of fuel used. These emissions factors vary for each pollutant by vessel type, vessel size, engine type, and fuel type \cite{olmer2017greenhouse, fourth2020greenhouse}. The IMO has validated this bottom-up approach against multiple independent data sources including: high-frequency continuous monitoring sensor data installed directly on vessels; vessel-level emissions data from the EU Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification emissions database; and other global emissions inventories \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. The IMO has also shown this bottom-up approach to be more reliable and substantially more granular than top-down approaches based solely on aggregate fuel consumption data \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. Consequently, the bottom-up approach has been widely adopted by the scientific community (a Google Scholar search in May 2025 for papers using the search term 'Automatic Identification System' 'emissions' yielded 6,290 results). 

This AIS-based method, however, still faces two fundamental challenges: 1) AIS coverage is incomplete and not fully representative of global maritime vessel activity; 2) key technical vessel characteristics necessary for bottom-up emissions modeling are often unknown, particularly for low-information vessels that are not listed in publicly available registries \cite{chen2024analysis}. First, AIS data only captures a fraction of vessel activity. Many vessels are not equipped with AIS systems or are not required to broadcast AIS \cite{taconet2019global}. Satellite imagery analysis has shown that about 75\% of industrial fishing vessels are not trackable by AIS, while over 25\% of vessels associated with transport (e.g., cargo, passenger) and offshore energy infrastructure (e.g. tankers, support vessels) are not tracked with AIS \cite{paolo2024satellite}. Even for vessels that do carry AIS, there can be gaps in data transmission due to challenges with the reception quality of satellites and terrestrial receivers, or due to intentional AIS disabling that may be associated with illegal activities or with legal activities such as hiding vessel locations in areas prone to piracy \cite{welch2022hot}. Changes in AIS adoption rate and the evolution of AIS technology over time also make it challenging to assess the trends of vessel activities using AIS data alone. For example, the introduction of Dynamic-AIS starting in 2022 has increased the AIS reception quality in some high traffic regions \cite{spire_documentation_2022}, resulting in significant increases in the vessel activity captured by AIS that do not necessarily reflect the trend in the actual amount of the activities.

Here we develop a method to estimate high-resolution marine vessel emissions globally from 2016 through 2024 by fusing AIS data from broadcasting vessels with Sentinel-1 (S1) Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) detections of non-broadcasting vessels. For over 900,000 AIS-broadcasting vessels, we quantify emissions for each vessel using precise latitude/longitude/timestamp information for over 97 billion individual AIS messages, which are broadcast at frequencies of minutes to hours. We do so using previously published and validated methods for the bottom-up emissions model itself \cite{olmer2017greenhouse, fourth2020greenhouse}, while enriching key vessel characteristics metadata for low-information vessels using machine learning models (main engine power, design speed, and vessel type). For non-broadcasting vessels, we leverage nearly \textbf{YYY (32?)} million S1 vessel detections to quantify emissions at a monthly gridded 1x1-degree  resolution and disaggregated by vessel size (across 10 length deciles) and vessel type (fishing or non-fishing). Our method leverages computing infrastructure and technical expertise developed by Global Fishing Watch \cite{gfw_website} 
 in processing AIS data \cite{kroodsma2018tracking} and S1 data \cite{paolo2024satellite}, and in estimating key vessel characteristics for vessels lacking readily available public information \cite{park2023tracking}. Our method, therefore, effectively addresses the two major challenges currently present in remotely measuring marine vessel emissions.

Our approach yields monthly, spatially explicit estimates of marine vessel emissions for three GHGs that drive climate change (CO$_{2}$, CH$_{4}$, N$_{2}$O) and six other air pollutants that impact local air quality and health (CO, NO$_{x}$, SO$_{x}$, PM$_{2.5}$, PM$_{10}$, VOC$_{s}$). Focusing our main results on CO$_{2}$ as the primary greenhouse gas (with other GHGs and air pollutants described in the supplement), we report trends in emissions from 2016 onwards, and compare trajectories of emission estimates based on either the AIS data alone or the fused AIS-S1 data. We compare the trend in marine vessel and terrestrial transportation CO$_{2}$ emissions over the same period. Our data also allows detailed mapping of emissions from vessel activities at a global scale with a breakdown across vessel class for non-fishing vessels (e.g., cargo and passenger) and fishing vessels (e.g., trawlers and squid jiggers).By comparing emission trends from AIS-only models and our fused AIS–SAR approach, we reveal the true magnitude and evolving dynamics of the marine sector’s contribution to global and regional emissions.

\section{Results}

To correctly estimate both the magnitude and trends of marine emissions, data fusion of AIS and Sentinel-1 is essential. If only considering emissions from vessels that broadcast AIS, in line with currently accepted state-of-the-art models, global CO$_{2}$ emissions in 2024 would be 1.1 billion MT (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}A), and the rate of change from 2016 to 2024 would show a 34\% increase (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}B). However, when including non-broadcasting vessels using S1 detections, global CO$_{2}$ emissions in 2024 were higher at 1.3  billion MT (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}A), and the trend from 2016 to 2024 is more modest at only a 22\% increase (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}B). Thus using only AIS data from broadcasting vessels \textit{underestimates} current global CO$_{2}$ emissions by 15\%, and \textit{overestimates} the increasing trend for 2016--2024 by 54\% (see Tables \ref{tab:total_percent_change_by_fleet} and \ref{tab:pollutant_ais_underestimation_overestimation_summary} for summaries of all other pollutants).

Fusing AIS and S1 data also provides a comprehensive picture of spatial marine vessel emissions patterns and how those patterns change over time (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}C and Fig. \ref{fig:fig-pollutant-maps}). Our data reveal clear spatial trends in how emissions have changed over time in major shipping lanes. These spatiotemporal data would allow for analysis of the GHG emissions that drive climate change, as well as of other air pollutants that impact local air quality and health. These analyses could inform the design and evaluation of interventions such as port regulations, fuel standards, carbon credits, and vessel speed reduction zones. They could also be used to quantify the spatial health impacts of air pollution related to shipping and fishing activity, along with the associated environmental justice implications.

Our high temporal resolution time series data reveal strong seasonal trends and event-driven variations in marine GHG emissions. We see consistent dips during major holidays including Christmas, New Year’s Day, and Chinese New Year, as well as during the COVID-19 pandemic (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-data-richness}A and Fig. \ref{fig:fig-pollutant-time-series}). Emissions from AIS-broadcasting vessels can be assigned to port stays and port-to-port voyages by analyzing the movement tracks of each individual vessel. China, United States, and Indonesia had the most CO$_{2}$ emissions during port stays in 2024 (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-data-richness}B).  Emissions can also be aggregated across various vessel characteristics, such as their vessel type or flag. Chemical and oil tankers, some of which transport fossil fuels including crude oil, are themselves the largest emitters, while trawlers are the highest-emitting type of fishing vessel (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-data-richness}C). Similar aggregations can be conducted at the flag level. Tracking individual vessel movement over time also supports analyses of emissions associated with specific vessels, specific port visits, and specific port-to-port trips. While detections from Sentinel-1 do not provide information on the characteristics of each vessel such as its flag, or allow for the tracking of individual vessels over time, the vessel length and type (fishing or non-fishing vessel) are inferred for each detection \cite{paolo2024satellite}. This enables monthly 1x1-degree emissions from non-broadcasting vessels to be aggregated by vessel size decile and/or whether they are fishing or non-fishing vessels.

Using the latest data available from the EU's Emissions Database for Global Atmospheric Research (EDGAR), we estimate that in 2023 marine vessels accounted for 15\% of all transportation-related CO$_{2}$ emissions (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-marine-terretrial}A). Marine vessel CO$_{2}$ emissions increased by 22\% between 2016 and 2023, five times faster than the growth rate of terrestrial transportation emissions (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-marine-terretrial}B, \cite{JRC138862}).

Total marine emission is determined by both the overall fleet size as well as the engine power and operating behavior of each individual vessel. We used a random forest model to predict CO$_2$ emissions based on the relative contributions of the following vessel features: total kW-hours, average speed, vessel size decile, and fishing/non-fishing vessel class (achieving a traditional $R^{2}$ of 0.99 for predicted versus observed CO$_2$ emissions). Our model results show that the increase in CO$_{2}$ emissions over 2016-2024 is primarily driven by increases in the total number of kW-hours, not by changes in operating speed (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-hours-speed-decomposition}A). Total kW-hours is itself determined by the total number of unique vessels, the number of hours each vessel operates, and the main engine power of each vessel.

\section{Discussion}

A high resolution picture of marine vessel emissions is essential to assess the impact of human activities on  the global climate and local population's health, and for designing effective policy solutions. However, incomplete vessel tracking and technical data processing challenges have hindered progress. Our proposed method overcomes these limitations by fusing two independent data sources and provides the first high-resolution view of the magnitude and trends of both greenhouse gas emissions and of other air pollutants from the majority of industrial marine vessels.  

Carbon markets could play a role in mitigating marine vessel emissions. The International Maritime Organization is calling for a global price on carbon for shipping emissions \cite{imo_carbon_price}. In recent years, the European Union Emissions Trading System has already been expanded to include marine transportation under the EU emissions cap, and to subject vessel activity to and from European Economic Area ports to a carbon price \cite{kotzampasakis2025maritime}. Our analysis shows that increasing emissions have largely been driven by an increase in the number of vessels (i.e., the extensive margin), not by increases in speed for those vessels (i.e., the intensive margin). This finding suggests that implementing a carbon market based on limiting emissions intensity (the amount of emissions per unit of activity or production) could be an effective strategy for mitigating marine vessel emissions, even with a growing number of vessels. Carbon markets that are based on carbon credits for emissions intensity rather than just on capping emissions, such as India's emerging carbon market \cite{gupta2023assessing}, allow for overall economic growth while still incentivizing more efficient activity. This is consistent with a review paper on current marine vessel emissions datasets, which concluded that measures aimed at increasing technical efficiency of ships could be an effective way to decouple emissions growth from global trade demand \cite{dengreview}.

How large is the climate externality associated with marine vessels? By multiplying the social cost of carbon (SCC, using 283 USD/MT \cite{moore2024synthesis}) with our estimated non-fishing vessel emissions for 2021 (1.2 billion MT CO$_{2}$ from cargo, tanker, etc vessel types), we estimate a global externality of 340 billion USD.  In other words, the present value cost of 2021 carbon emissions from the marine transportation sector was about 340 billion USD, which represents about 5\% of the total value of all goods transported by maritime trade (using 6.63 trillion USD as the value of maritime-traded goods \cite{hoffmeister2022developing}). Using a similar approach, our voyage-level emissions dataset could be used to estimate the SCC for country-to-country trade flows, allowing for the calculation of country-to-country metrics such as the SCC per USD value of goods traded, or the SCC per metric tonne of goods traded. In principle, for vessels tracked with AIS, it is also possible to assign an externality cost to individual port-to-port trips. 

Our approach provides an example of how to resolve a more general challenge in environmental monitoring: the temporal trend in an environmental variable is often confounded by changes in monitoring itself. For marine vessel emissions, this challenge manifests as an increase in adoption of AIS and number of vessels being tracked over time, which lead current methods to overestimate the rate of growth of at-sea emissions (by 54\%). Similarly, more extensive monitoring of other environmental variables could lead us to overestimate their growth rates (for example, for invasive species discoveries \cite{costello2003pattern}).  Our approach fusing independent data sources is robust to changes in monitoring effort over time, and could be applied in other settings to produce unbiased estimates of environmental change.

\section*{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{Data description}

This work relies on multiple sources of spatial and tabular data to assess marine vessel emissions globally. We use AIS data, vessel registry information, and Sentinel-1 SAR derived data to construct our AIS-based emission model and the non-broadcasting emissions model (Fig. \ref{fig:gfw_emissions_framework_flowchart}).

\paragraph{AIS data sets}

We use individual AIS messages (pings) from the latest version of GFW’s AIS pipeline v3 (citation needed), which parses, cleans, and augments raw AIS data (Kroodsma et al., 2018). To minimize noise, we include only AIS messages that occur within valid segments and within daily segments that do not overlap. This dataset also classified whether a fishing vessel is actively engaged in fishing activities at the ping level. Additionally, we use the GFW voyage table, which contains information on port-to-port voyages, and the port visits table, allowing us to aggregate ping-level emissions to the voyage and port-visit levels.

\paragraph{Vessel information data}

We use vessel characteristics data from GFW's vessel metadata tables. For each vessel, this includes: (1) official registry information when available (Park et al., 2023), or (2) algorithm-derived vessel characteristics—including vessel class, main engine power, and gross tonnage—when registry data are unavailable (Kroodsma et al., 2018). For vessels classified as “cargo”, we implement a Random Forest classification sub-model that builds off the general vessel classification algorithm to differentiate cargo vessel sub categories that align with IMO cargo vessel class types. The model is trained on port visit patterns from over 26,000 vessels with known IMO cargo vessel class types. Sequences of port visits are converted into usable model features using a Word2Vec model. Additional model features include vessel activity metrics such as port hours, average distance from shore, and average speed. With this approach, we can differentiate vessels that were previously grouped as an undifferentiated cargo vessel type into specific categories, including bulk carrier, container, general, refrigerated, and ro-ro. This refinement allows us to more accurately assign auxiliary engine power, boiler power, and design speed. As a result, we constructed a vessel characteristics dataset consisting of over 900,000 active AIS-broadcasting vessels.

\paragraph{Sentinel-1 SAR data set}

For our non-broadcasting fleet emission estimates, we use vessel detections from Sentinel-1 (S1) Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) imagery. The GFW dataset provides spatiotemporal information on each detection, including inferred vessel length and the likelihood of being classified as either a fishing vessel or non-fishing vessel. By identifying AIS-broadcasting vessels that are spatiotemporally matched to S1 detections, the dataset categorizes each detection as either a non-broadcasting vessel or an AIS-broadcasting vessel (Kroodsma et al. 2022). Additionally, detections are filtered to exclude repeated detections of vessels in anchorages, offshore infrastructure, potential ice, and other other ambiguities, such as the radar aliasing effect (Paolo et al. 2024). As a result, we obtained a dataset containing 31,835,861 vessel detections from 2016 to 2024, of which 18,823,780 were matched to a broadcasting vessel.

\subsection{Building the AIS-based emissions model}

We adapted our AIS-based model from the methodologies described in the 2017 ICCT study on global shipping emissions (Olmer et al., 2017) and the 2020 IMO Fourth Greenhouse Gas Study (Faber and Xing, 2020), making modifications to better align with the structure and characteristics of our data sets. From a high-level, we: 1) determine the main engine energy use, auxiliary engine energy use, and boiler energy use for each individual AIS message based on dynamic vessel movement and static vessel characteristic data; 2) multiply the engine and boiler energy use numbers by pollutant- and engine-type-specific emissions factors in order to determine the amount of emissions for six pollutants associated with the engines and boiler for each ping; 3) add up the emissions for these six pollutants across the engines and boiler, to obtain the total emissions for each ping and each of these six pollutants; 4) estimate emissions for three additional pollutants that do not detailed information on emissions factors by simply multiplying the total CO$_{2}$ emissions for each ping by a conversion factor \cite{mayesshipping}.

Main engine energy use is estimated based on the hours between with each AIS ping and the previous ping, the main engine power from the vessel characteristics dataset, and a load factor used to adjust the estimate. The load factor, along with the correction factors that define it, is described in Olmer et al. (2017) and Faber and Xing (2020). These correction factors account for weather conditions—represented by distance to shore, a proxy that can be observed from AIS data—resistance due to fouling, and vessel draft. Additionally, a speed-power correction factor is applied to weight fuel consumption based on the vessel’s speed relative to its design speed. The definition of this factor, along with the design speeds, was extracted from Faber and Xing (2020) and assigned based on vessel class and size category. To determine the vessel speed associated with each AIS message, we use either: 1) the transmitted instantaneous speed associated with the AIS message if less than an hour has passed since the last message was transmitted; or 2) the implied speed for messages for which more than an hour has passed since the previous message (calculated as the distance traveled divided by the elapsed time between the two messages). For fishing vessels, we adjust the main engine load factor so that it falls between 0.2 and 0.9, and assign a main engine load factor of 0.75 to trawlers and dredge fishing vessels that are actively fishing due to the energy intensive nature of their fishing operations \cite{coello2015ais, sala2018economics}.

In accordance with Faber and Xing, 2020, our model also accounts for differences in auxiliary engine energy use and boiler energy use depending on the vessel’s operational phase—whether it is at berth, at anchor, maneuvering, or at sea. These phases are identified using AIS speed, distance to port, and distance to the coast. Where applicable, power outputs for auxiliary engines and boilers, based on vessel class and size, are extracted from Faber and Xing (2020) and are applied accordingly for each phase.

After calculating energy use, we apply pollutant-specific emission factors to the main engine, auxiliary engine, and boiler for six pollutants (CO$_{2}$, CH$_{4}$, CO, NO$_{x}$, SO$_{x}$, N$_{2}$O), following Olmer et al. (2017). These factors are each averaged from across the relevant pollutant factors associated with SSD, MSD, and HSD engine types, which together represent approximately 98\% of vessels engines (Faber and Xing, 2020). To get total emissions for each AIS message (i.e., emissions from the previous ping in a segment to the current one), we sum emissions from both engines and boilers. For VOCs, PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$, which do not have as detailed information on emissions factors, we instead multiply the CO$_{2}$ emissions by pollutant-specific conversion factors \cite{mayesshipping}.

\subsection{Building the non-broadcasting emissions model}

We estimate emissions from the non-broadcasting fleet by spatiotemporally extrapolating AIS-based emissions to non-broadcasting vessels, leveraging vessel detections from S1 SAR data. S1 imagery enables identification and quantification of both broadcasting and non-broadcasting vessels at specific locations and times, and also allows for the estimation of inferred vessel length for every detection \cite{paolo2024satellite}. 

We define a global spatial resolution of 1x1-degree pixels and use monthly aggregation units for temporal resolution. We divide vessels into 10 size classes according to length deciles derived from all S1 detections unmatched with AIS that occurred from 2016 to 2024. These length-based decile thresholds are consistently applied to both AIS-derived vessel characteristics and Sentinel-1 detected vessel lengths in order to bin all AIS-broadcasting vessels and all unmatched S1 detections into length bins (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-s1-ais-size-class-comparison}). Using this classification, we perform extrapolations of AIS-based emissions to non-broadcasting vessels within the same length groups, vessel type (either fishing or non-fishing vessels), and by space and time. We extrapolate based on length bins because inferred length is available for all S1 detections, length is representative of main engine power (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-ais-length-power-relationship}), and vessels with larger engines emit more all-else-equal. We extrapolate based on vessel type (fishing or non-fishing) because some fishing vessel types have fundamentally different emissions profiles than non-fishing vessels (i.e., trawlers and dredgers emit more at low speeds due to the nature of their energy-intensive gear deployment, which is reflected in their gear-specific main engine load factor adjustment.)

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig-s1-ais-size-class-comparison-1.png}
    \caption{Length distributions for each vessel type (non-fishing and fishing) and length bin decile, separately for AIS-broadcasting vessels and unmatched S1 detections. The point represents the median value, the vertical bars represent the interquartile range (i.e., 25th percentile and 75th percentile), and the lightly colored horizonal lines represents the full range of values.}
    \label{fig:fig-s1-ais-size-class-comparison}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig-ais-length-power-relationship-1.png}
    \caption{The relationship between main engine power (kilowatts) and length (meters), where each point is a specific AIS-broadcasting vessel. The relationship is shown for non-fishing vessels and fishing vessels.}
    \label{fig:fig-ais-length-power-relationship}
\end{figure}

For each pixel, month, vessel type, and vessel size bin, we use S1 data to determine: (1) the number of vessel detections matched to AIS-broadcasting vessels; (2) the number of vessel detections not matched to AIS-broadcasting vessels (i.e., non-broadcasting vessels); and (3) the ratio of non-broadcasting vessels to AIS-broadcasting vessels (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-dark-vessel-fraction-detected-time-series}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig-dark-vessel-fraction-detected-time-series-1.png}
    \caption{Fraction of S1-detected vessels matched AIS-broadcasting vessels over time, disaggregated by vessel type and length size class.}
    \label{fig:fig-dark-vessel-fraction-detected-time-series}
\end{figure}

S1 coverage is constrained by the satellite’s orbital path and the presence of land, resulting in limited observations in some coastal areas and most of the open ocean. To estimate vessel ratios for pixels outside the S1 footprint, we apply a k-nearest neighbors (KNN) imputation approach. Specifically, for each month, vessel type, and vessel size class, we identify the 20 closest pixels with a non-missing S1 ratio and assign to each missing pixel the mean non-missing ratio from its nearest neighbors (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-map-dark-to-ais-s1-ratio}). Additionally, within the S1 footprint, there may be pixels for which no detections match AIS-broadcasting vessels in a given month, vessel type, and size class, resulting in null ratios. In these cases, we assign the mean ratio derived from a similar KNN extrapolation, except using only the eight nearest spatially adjacent pixels, accounting for immediate neighboring pixels if intersecting, and to limit the influence of more distant ones otherwise.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig-map-dark-to-ais-s1-ratio-1.png}
    \caption{Mean ratio value within and outside S1 footprint, aggregated across all years, and vessel lengths, for representation purposes. Model inputs are disaggregated by pixel, month, vessel class and length bin.}
    \label{fig:fig-map-dark-to-ais-s1-ratio}
\end{figure}

We estimate emissions from non-broadcasting vessels by multiplying AIS-based emissions by the ratio of non-broadcasting to AIS-broadcasting vessels for each pollutant, pixel, month, vessel type, and vessel size bin (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-global-annual-co2-dark-emissions-by-type}). To select the appropriate ratio, we apply the following hierarchy: (1) where available, we use the precise monthly pixel-level ratio derived from Sentinel-1 data; or (2) if the precise pixel-level ratio is not available, we use the the imputed KNN-derived ratio. Across the entire 2016 to 2024 time series, 68\% of non-broadcasting emissions were estimated based on inferred ratios derived from the KNN approach (71\% for non-fishing vessels, and 39\% for fishing vessels).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig-global-annual-co2-dark-emissions-by-type-1.png}
    \caption{Summary of global annual CO$_{2}$ non-broadcasting emissions over time, disaggregated by vessel type.}
    \label{fig:fig-global-annual-co2-dark-emissions-by-type}
\end{figure}

This approach provides corresponding non-broadcasting fleet emissions estimates for every month and location where AIS-based estimates are available. Adding both values gives us monthly gridded total emissions estimates from both AIS-broadcasting and non-broadcasting fleets.

\subsection{Validating non-broadcasting emissions model}

Given the novelty of our methods, there is no existing validation dataset for measured emissions from the non-broadcasting fleet against which to validate our estimates. Therefore, we use the AIS-based model results to simulate the likely performance of the S1-based emissions model, treating a subset of AIS-matched detections from S1 data as simulated proxies for non-broadcasting vessels. We focus on assessing the performance of CO$_{2}$ emissions estimates, although the performance of other pollutants will be very similar since all pollutant emissions are highly correlated.

We aggregate vessel-level AIS-based CO$_{2}$ emissions to match the analysis unit used for non-broadcasting emissions (i.e., aggregating emissions across vessels for a 1x1-degree spatial grids by month, by vessel type—fishing and non-fishing—and by 10 vessel size bins). Using the actual observed S1 detections, we quantify the overall fraction of detections matched to AIS (a derivation of the data shown in Fig. \ref{fig:fig-dark-vessel-fraction-detected-time-series}). Based on these fractions, we randomly assign a portion of each AIS-broadcasting vessel type and size class to the “training” dataset, while the remaining AIS-broadcasting vessels are allocated to the “testing” dataset. The training dataset represents AIS-broadcasting vessels, while the testing dataset simulates non-broadcasting fleet vessels. So for example, for the largest size class non-fishing vessels, the observed data show that 93.3\% of S1 detections were matched to AIS-broadcasting vessels. To generate our simulated ‘testing’ and ‘training’ datasets for the largest non-fishing vessel size class, we therefore randomly assign 93.3\% of the non-fishing large vessels from the AIS dataset to be in the simulated ‘training’ dataset and 6.7\% of these vessels to be in the simulated ‘testing’ dataset.

Following the same procedure as for the primary non-broadcast fleet emissions model, we calculate the ratio of simulated non-broadcasting fleet vessels to simulated AIS-broadcasting vessels within each analysis unit inside the S1 footprint. Outside the footprint, we apply the ratio observed across all covered pixels for a given month, vessel type, and size class. We then estimate total emissions from the training dataset vessels and multiply this value by the ratio of simulated non-broadcasting  vessels to simulated AIS vessels to obtain the predicted emissions from the simulated non-broadcasting fleet.

Since the simulated non-broadcasting vessels actually broadcast AIS, we can also calculate their observed emissions from the AIS-based emissions model for each analysis unit. By comparing the predicted simulated non-broadcasting emissions with the observed emissions for each analysis unit, we can assess the overall performance which yields an $ R^2 $ of 0.98.

\subsection{Decomposition analysis}

In order to better understand the drivers of emissions trends, we perform a decomposition analysis to assess the relative contributions of total vessel kilowatt-hours and verage vessel speed. To do so, we first construct a dataset of annual total hours, total kW-hours, and total distance traveled, aggregated across both the AIS-broadcasting and non-broadcasting vessels, and disaggregated by vessel type (fishing and non-fishing), and decile length bins, for 2016-2024. To build this dataset, for AIS-broadcasting vessels we simply use the AIS-based emissions estimates as described above for the main analysis. For non-broadcasting vessels, we extrapolate total hours, total kW-hours, and total distance traveled in the exact same way as we extrapolate emissions for the main analysis. We do so by simply multiplying AIS-based total hours, total kW-hours, and total distance traveled by the ratio of non-broadcasting to AIS-broadcasting vessels for each pixel, month, vessel type, and vessel decile bin. We then aggregate the monthly non-broadcasting hours, kW-hours, and distance traveled to an annual time scale, and then add these values to the corresponding AIS-broadcasting values to get total numbers aggregated across both AIS-broadcasting and non-broadcasting vessels. Finally, we calculate average speed for each year (in knots), vessel type, and decile length bin by dividing the corresponding total distance traveled (in nautical miles) by the total hours. For each year, vessel type, and decile length bin we also take the total CO$_{2}$ emissions as estimated in the main analysis.

With this dataset, we next train and test a random forest regression model to predict CO$_{2}$ emissions for each  year, vessel type, and decile length bin as a function of total kW-hours, average speed, vessel type, and decile length bin as a categorical feature. We do so using the the \emph{tidymodels} suite of R machine learning packages \cite{tidymodels}. We first split the dataset into a training dataset (using 80\% of the observations) and a holdout testing dataset (using 20\% of the observations). Using the training dataset, we use the the \emph{ranger} R package \cite{ranger} to train a random forest model \cite{breiman2001random} with 1000 trees, a minimum node size of 5, and an $mtry$ value of 2 (the minimum number of variables used to split each node; we chose this as the square root of the number of model features, which is 4). With the trained model, we then test the model performance using the testing dataset by comparing the predicted annual CO$_{2}$ emissions to the actual CO$_{2}$ emissions. We do so by using a traditional $R^2$ metric which uses sum of squares and allows for negative values (as opposed to using an $R^2$ value based on correlation, which forces a value between 0 and 1) \cite{yardstick}. This yields a more conservation estimate of performance. Finally, with the model performance now estimated, we train the final model using the entire dataset (combining both the training dataset and testing dataset). This final model is then used for scenario predictions.

Using the final trained model, we make predictions for four different scenarios:

1. Observed kW-hours and observed speed. Here we simply use the dataset as-is, and make predictions for CO$_{2}$. This scenario corresponds to the world as we have observed it.

2. Observed kW-hours and constant speed. Here we use the observed kW-hours as-is, but set the speed at constant values corresponding to what they were in 2016 (by vessel class and length bin decile).

3. Constant kW-hours and observed speed. Here we use the observed speed as-is, but set the kW-hours at constant values corresponding to what they were in 2016 (by vessel class and length bin decile).

4. Constant kW-hours and constant speed. Here we set both the kW-hours and speed at constant values corresponding to what they were in 2016 (by vessel class and length bin decile).

In this way, (1) reconstructs predictions for our observed data. On the other end of the spectrum, (4) constructs a scenario for what the world would have looked like if neither kW-hours or speed had changed over time. The difference between predictions from (2) and (4) isolate the impact of changing kW-hours over time. Finally, the difference in predictions from (3) and (4) isolates the impact of changing speed over time.

\subsection{Disentangling trends in AIS-based emissions data: the introduction of Dynamic AIS}

AIS messages are broadcast from vessels and then received through one of three technologies: satellite-based receivers, terrestrial receivers, and dynamic receivers. Satellite and terrestrial receivers are the oldest receiver types, while 2022 saw the widespread adoption of 'Dynamic-AIS' in several regions throughout the world. Dynamic AIS is a technology that is implemented by Spire, one of the major AIS data providers, in which satellite-enabled AIS receivers are installed on vessels that travel through some of the world's busiest shipping lanes  such as the South China Sea \cite{spire_documentation_2022}. Through GFW, we can determine the receiver type for every single AIS message in the dataset over time. In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}, we summarize the amount of estimated emissions and the number of distinct vessels that were detected by the three different types of AIS receivers. In 2022, we can clearly see a sharp rise in the amount of emissions that were estimated from Dynamic AIS, which intuitively coincides with a decrease in the emissions that are estimated from satellite and terrestrial receivers. On aggregate, this led to an increase in total estimated emissions. The number of distinct vessels detected by dynamic AIS also sharply rose in 2022, almost equaling the number of vessels detected by all receiver types.

In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type-top-flags}, we see the amount of quantified emissions for the top 10 vessel flags broken apart by AIS receiver type. This is likely driven by whether or not each flag travels through regions where Dynamic AIS was widely adopted. It is important to note that towards the end of 2021, China experience a blackout of terrestrial AIS signals due to new privacy laws that were being implemented (\url{https://veson.com/blog/benefits-of-dynamic-ais-for-maritime-data-in-times-of-uncertainty/}). This sharp decline from 2021 to 2022 in terrestrial-based emissions is clearly evident for China. Without the introduction of Dynamic AIS, it is possible that this disruption may have resulted in erroneously small emissions in China following the terrestrial receiver blackout. This is another example of how multiple complementary technologies can be fused in order to maximize emissions coverage over time.
