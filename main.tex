\begin{abstract}

 Emissions from the transportation sector are well understood on land, but are less well understood in the oceans because of the often opaque nature of vessel activity at sea. Understanding the magnitude and trends of ocean-based greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and non-GHG pollutant emissions is the first step toward mitigating them. We develop a method for quantifying high-resolution spatiotemporal  ocean-based vessel emissions at a global scale by fusing two unique satellite-based data sources: 1) Automatic Identification System (AIS) to estimate vessel-level emissions for the over 665,000 boats that broadcast their positions using AIS; and 2) synthetic aperture radar (SAR) data from the Sentinel-1 satellite constellation to estimate emissions for boats that do not use AIS. We find that vessels emitted approximately 1.3 billion metric tons of CO2 in 2023, accounting for 15\% of global fossil fuel emissions from the transportation sector. We also find that ocean-based CO2 emissions increased by 24\% between 2016 and 2023, more than five times the increase measured from land-based transportation emissions. Data fusion is critical to getting these numbers right: if only AIS data were used, the currently accepted state-of-the-art for measuring vessel emissions, global emissions in 2024 would be \textit{underestimated} by 14\%, and the increasing trend from 2016 to 2024 would be \textit{overestimated} by 41\%. Our approach provides a robust and rich dataset that can be leveraged for a variety of policy- and market-based emissions mitigation interventions. It also provides an example of how data fusion can disentangle actual environmental changes from changes in monitoring in order to more accurately quantify both the magnitude and trends of environmental problems.

\end{abstract}

\subsection{Disentangling trends in AIS-based emissions data}
\subsubsection{The introduction of Dynamic AIS}

AIS messages are broadcast from vessels and then received through one of three technologies: satellite-based receivers, terrestrial receivers, and dynamic receivers. Satellite and terrestrial receivers are the oldest receiver types, while 2022 saw the widespread adoption of 'Dynamic-AIS' in several regions throughout the world. Dynamic AIS is a technology that is implemented by Spire, one of the major AIS data providers, in which satellite-enabled AIS receivers are installed on vessels that travel through some of the world's busiest shipping lanes  such as the South China Sea (\href{https://documentation.spire.com/ais-fundamentals/ais-data-sources/dynamic-ais/}{source}). Through GFW, we can determine the receiver type for every single AIS message in the dataset over time. In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}, we summarize the amount of estimated emissions and the number of distinct vessels that were detected by the three different types of AIS receivers. In 2022, we can clearly see a sharp rise in the amount of emissions that were measured by Dynamic AIS, which intuitively coincides with a decrease in the emissions that are measured from satellite and terrestrial receivers. On aggregate, this led to an increase in total measured emissions. The number of distinct vessels detected by dynamic AIS also sharply rose in 2022, almost equaling the number of vessels detected by all receiver types.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{qmd/quantifying_ghg_emissions_files/figure-pdf/fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type-1.png}
    \caption{Annual total CO2 emissions (metric tonnes) by the AIS-broadcasting fleet, disaggregated by AIS receiver type (satellite, terrestrial, and dynamic) and total aggregated across receiver types. A dashed vertical line is shown for 2022, the first year in which dynamic AIS was widely adopted.}
    \label{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}
\end{figure}

In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type-top-flags}, we see the amount of quantified emissions for the top 10 vessel flags broken apart by AIS receiver type (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-unique-vessels-by-ais-receiver-type-top-flag} similarly shows trends in unique vessels detected by each receiver type). The trends are similar to those seen on aggregate ( Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}), although there is variation in terms of the increase in Dynamic AIS detections across different flags. This is likely driven by whether or not each flag travels through regions where Dynamic AIS was widely adopted. It is important to note that towards the end of 2021, China experience a blackout of terrestrial AIS signals due to new privacy laws that were being implemented (\href{https://veson.com/blog/benefits-of-dynamic-ais-for-maritime-data-in-times-of-uncertainty/}{source). This sharp decline from 2021 to 2022 in terrestrial-based emissions is clearly evident for China. Without the introduction of Dynamic AIS, it is possible that this disruption may have resulted in erroneously small emissions in China following the terrestrial receiver blackout. This is another example of how multiple complementary technologies can be fused in order to maximize emissions coverage over time.

\subsubsection{Changes in fleet composition and vessel behavior}

In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-unique-vessels-hours-power},  we see that the number of unique AIS-broadcasting vessels is steadily increasing over time, showing an 78\% increase from 2015 to 2024. The total operational hours of these vessels increase 104\% over this time period, while the total main engine power for these vessels saw a 37\% increase. This provides some evidence that a primary driver of increasing observed emissions at a global scale (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-emissions-by-fleet}) could be the increasing number of vessels that broadcast AIS.

In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-unique-vessels-by-earliest-operation-year},  we look at how each of these indicators would change over time if we only included the vessels that have been operating since a given earliest operation year. For example, for an earliest operation year of 2015, we can see how CO2 emissions change over time for just those vessels that have been operating since 2015. Regardless of which year we take as a baseline, the emissions for the fleet from any given year shows a consistent decreasing pattern from the first year to the last year. This is also seen in the number of broadcasting vessels, operating hours, and total main engine power. This provides further evidence that the main source of increasing observed emissions seen across all vessels at a global scale (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-emissions-by-fleet}) is primarily driven by the increasing number of vessels that broadcast AIS over time (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-unique-vessels-hours-power}).

The relationship between hours and speeds may be misleading, as the dataset includes information from both trips and port stays. While hours accumulate regardless of whether a vessel is on a trip or at port, distances covered during port stays are minimal, leading to misrepresented speed values.

To address this, we can assess the actual changes in these parameters (considering distances as well) by exclusively analyzing emissions from trips. Below, we present data extracted from the trip emissions dataset, aggregated by vessel for all trips starting in a given year (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-all-years-operating-vessels-norm-mean-values-from-trip-level-emissions-1}). This shows a completely different trend compared to the observations above. Restricting vessel information to those that has a registered at least one trip in each year has moved the number of vessels considered in this representation from 101754 to 67715.
