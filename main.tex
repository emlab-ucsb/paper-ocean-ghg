\begin{abstract}

 Emissions from the transportation sector are well understood on land, but are less well understood in the oceans because of the often opaque nature of vessel activity at sea. Understanding the magnitude and trends of ocean-based greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and non-GHG pollutant emissions is the first step toward mitigating them. We develop a method for quantifying high-resolution spatiotemporal  ocean-based vessel emissions at a global scale by fusing two unique satellite-based data sources: 1) Automatic Identification System (AIS) to estimate vessel-level emissions for the over 665,000 boats that broadcast their positions using AIS; and 2) synthetic aperture radar (SAR) data from the Sentinel-1 satellite constellation to estimate emissions for boats that do not use AIS. We find that vessels emitted approximately 1.3 billion metric tons of $CO_{2}$ in 2023, accounting for 15\% of global fossil fuel emissions from the transportation sector. We also find that ocean-based $CO_{2}$ emissions increased by 24\% between 2016 and 2023, more than five times the increase measured from land-based transportation emissions. Data fusion is critical to getting these numbers right: if only AIS data were used, the currently accepted state-of-the-art for measuring vessel emissions, global emissions in 2024 would be \textit{underestimated} by 14\%, and the increasing trend from 2016 to 2024 would be \textit{overestimated} by 41\%. Our approach provides a robust and rich dataset that can be leveraged for a variety of policy- and market-based emissions mitigation interventions. It also provides an example of how data fusion can disentangle actual environmental changes from changes in monitoring in order to more accurately quantify both the magnitude and trends of environmental problems.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The transportation sector is a major source of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and contributor to global climate change. In 2023, the transportation sector was responsible for 21\% of all carbon dioxide ($CO_{2}$) emissions globally (\cite{JRC138862})...

Current methods for estimating emissions at sea rely on vessel tracking data from the Automatic Identification System (AIS) (\cite{olmer2017greenhouse, fourth2020greenhouse}), but cannot provide comprehensive coverage due to the often opaque nature of vessel activity in the oceans. Analysis of satellite imagery has shown that upwards of 76\% of industrial fishing vessels are not tracked by AIS, while upwards of 30\% of transport and energy vessels are not tracked (\cite{paolo2024satellite})...

Here we develop a method for comprehensively estimating ocean-based vessel emissions that fuses both AIS data for vessels that broadcast their positions and Sentinel-1 Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) for non-broadcasting vessels...

\section{Results}

In 2023 there were 1.3 billion metric tonnes (MT) of ocean-based transportation $CO_{2}$ emissions, which accounted for 15\% of all transportation-related $CO_{2}$ emissions (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-emissions-by-data-source}A). Ocean-based transportation $CO_{2}$ emissions increased by 24\% between 2016 and 2023, an increase 5.8 times larger than land-based transportation emissions (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-emissions-by-data-source}A). To correctly estimate both the magnitude and trends of ocean-based emissions, data fusion of AIS and Sentinel-1 is necessary (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-emissions-by-data-source}B). When only including emissions based on AIS, global emissions in 2024 were 1.1 billion MT, and the trend from 2016 to 2024 shows a 34\% increase. When also including non-broadcasting vessels using Sentinel-1 detections, global emissions in 2024 were 1.3  billion MT, and the trend from 2016 to 2024 is more modest at only a 24\% increase. This suggests that if only using AIS data, global emissions in 2024 would be underestimated by 14\%, and the increasing trend from 2016 to 2024 would be overestimated by 41\%.

\section{Discussion}

Accurately and comprehensively measuring emissions across both space and time is necessary for designing emissions mitigation interventions...

Our approach provides an example of how to resolve a more general challenge that is common across all environmental monitoring: understanding the trends of environmental problems is critical, but true trends of the problem can be confounded by simultaneous changes in the quality of the monitoring itself. As environmental monitoring generally improves over time with improved technology and sampling, this leads to the potential for overestimating the increasing trend of environmental problems. This monitoring challenge has been documented in other environmental domains, including the study of introduced nonindigenous species (\cite{costello2003pattern})...

\subsection{Disentangling trends in AIS-based emissions data}
\subsubsection{The introduction of Dynamic AIS}

AIS messages are broadcast from vessels and then received through one of three technologies: satellite-based receivers, terrestrial receivers, and dynamic receivers. Satellite and terrestrial receivers are the oldest receiver types, while 2022 saw the widespread adoption of 'Dynamic-AIS' in several regions throughout the world. Dynamic AIS is a technology that is implemented by Spire, one of the major AIS data providers, in which satellite-enabled AIS receivers are installed on vessels that travel through some of the world's busiest shipping lanes  such as the South China Sea (\href{https://documentation.spire.com/ais-fundamentals/ais-data-sources/dynamic-ais/}{source}). Through GFW, we can determine the receiver type for every single AIS message in the dataset over time. In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}, we summarize the amount of estimated emissions and the number of distinct vessels that were detected by the three different types of AIS receivers. In 2022, we can clearly see a sharp rise in the amount of emissions that were measured by Dynamic AIS, which intuitively coincides with a decrease in the emissions that are measured from satellite and terrestrial receivers. On aggregate, this led to an increase in total measured emissions. The number of distinct vessels detected by dynamic AIS also sharply rose in 2022, almost equaling the number of vessels detected by all receiver types.

A dashed vertical line is shown for 2022, the first year in which dynamic AIS was widely adopted.}

In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type-top-flags}, we see the amount of quantified emissions for the top 10 vessel flags broken apart by AIS receiver type. The trends are similar to those seen on aggregate ( Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}), although there is variation in terms of the increase in Dynamic AIS detections across different flags. This is likely driven by whether or not each flag travels through regions where Dynamic AIS was widely adopted. It is important to note that towards the end of 2021, China experience a blackout of terrestrial AIS signals due to new privacy laws that were being implemented (\href{https://veson.com/blog/benefits-of-dynamic-ais-for-maritime-data-in-times-of-uncertainty/}{source). This sharp decline from 2021 to 2022 in terrestrial-based emissions is clearly evident for China. Without the introduction of Dynamic AIS, it is possible that this disruption may have resulted in erroneously small emissions in China following the terrestrial receiver blackout. This is another example of how multiple complementary technologies can be fused in order to maximize emissions coverage over time.
