\begin{abstract}

 Understanding the magnitude and trends of ocean-based greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and non-GHG pollutant emissions is the first step toward mitigating them. We develop a method for quantifying high-resolution spatiotemporal  ocean-based vessel emissions at a global scale by fusing two unique satellite-based data sources: 1) Automatic Identification System (AIS) to estimate vessel-level emissions for the over 665,000 boats that broadcast their positions using AIS; and 2) synthetic aperture radar (SAR) data from the Sentinel-1 satellite constellation to estimate emissions for boats that do not use AIS. We find that industrial vessels emitted approximately 1.3 billion metric tons of CO2 in 2024, accounting for 3.5\% of global fossil fuel emissions. We also find that vessel CO2 emissions increased by 23\% between 2016 and 2024, roughly three times the rate of global fossil fuel emissions. Data fusion is critical to getting these numbers right: if only AIS data were used, the currently accepted state-of-the-art, global emissions in 2024 would be underestimated by 17\%, and the increasing trend from 2016 to 2024 would be overestimated by 48\%. Our approach provides a robust and rich dataset that can be leveraged for a variety of policy- and market-based emissions mitigation interventions. It also provides an example of how data fusion can disentangle actual environmental changes from changes in monitoring in order to more accurately quantify both the magnitude and trends of environmental problems.

\end{abstract}

\subsubsection{Changes in fleet composition and vessel behavior}

In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-unique-vessels-hours-power},  we see that the number of unique AIS-broadcasting vessels is steadily increasing over time, showing an 78\% increase from 2015 to 2024. The total operational hours of these vessels increase 104\% over this time period, while the total main engine power for these vessels saw a 37\% increase. This provides some evidence that a primary driver of increasing observed emissions at a global scale (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-emissions-by-fleet}) could be the increasing number of vessels that broadcast AIS.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{qmd/quantifying_ghg_emissions_files/figure-pdf/fig-total-annual-unique-vessels-hours-power-1.png}
    \caption{Annual total CO2 emissions (metric tonnes); total operating hours; total engine power across vessels (killowatts); number distinct operating vessels; and average speed; across all AIS-broadcasting vessels. The percent change from 2015 to 2024 is shown next to each line..}
    \label{fig:fig-total-annual-unique-vessels-hours-power}
\end{figure}

In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-unique-vessels-by-earliest-operation-year},  we look at how each of these indicators would change over time if we only included the vessels that have been operating since a given earliest operation year. For example, for an earliest operation year of 2015, we can see how CO2 emissions change over time for just those vessels that have been operating since 2015. Regardless of which year we take as a baseline, the emissions for the fleet from any given year shows a consistent decreasing pattern from the first year to the last year. This is also seen in the number of broadcasting vessels, operating hours, and total main engine power. This provides further evidence that the main source of increasing observed emissions seen across all vessels at a global scale (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-emissions-by-fleet}) is primarily driven by the increasing number of vessels that broadcast AIS over time (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-unique-vessels-hours-power}).
