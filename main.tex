\begin{abstract} \bfseries \boldmath

 Increased concentrations of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere due to human activities trap heat and cause the planet to warm, leading to changes in the global climate. The transportation sector contributes significantly towards these emissions through land-based emissions and ocean-based emissions. Yet despite advances in marine vessel tracking technology and emission inventories, a substantial and unquantified fraction of vessel activity remains invisible in current global datasets.  As a result, global inventories chronically underestimate marine vessel emissions and misrepresent trends, undermining efforts to monitor, regulate, and mitigate their climate impacts. We developed a method for quantifying marine vessel emissions at global scale by fusing two independent types of satellite data: vessel tracking using the Automatic Identification System (AIS) to estimate vessel-level emissions, and vessel detection using synthetic aperture radar (SAR) imagery to estimate emissions for vessels that do not use AIS. We found that vessels emitted approximately 1.3 billion metric tonnes of CO$_{2}$ in 2023, accounting for 14\% of global fossil fuel emissions from the transportation sector. We also found that marine vessel CO$_{2}$ emissions increased by 23\% between 2017 and 2023, more than 13 times the increase in land-based transportation emissions. Because many vessels do not broadcast AIS, current approaches solely based on AIS \textit{underestimate} the magnitude of 2024 emissions by 9\%. Meanwhile, because AIS coverage has generally been increasing over time, current global estimates \textit{overestimate} the increasing emissions trend by 20\% (from 2017 to 2024). Our approach provides a robust estimate of the magnitude, trends, and spatial patterns of marine vessel GHG emissions and non-GHG air pollutant emissions. This information can serve as a key input to designing and evaluating interventions aimed at mitigating climate change.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The transportation sector is a major source of global greenhouse gas (GHG) and non-GHG air pollution emissions, responsible for 21\% of all CO$_{2}$ emissions globally in 2023 \cite{JRC138862}. While estimation of land-based transportation emissions is well understood, fundamental gaps remain in the estimation of marine vessel emissions. These emissions come primarily from the transportation of goods via shipping vessels, carriage of passengers, and fishing activity, with a smaller contribution from other industrial activities such as dredging and offshore infrastructure maintenance. Because these diverse vessel types vary dramatically in the extent to which vessel behavior is tracked and vessel characteristics are understood, we lack a comprehensive estimate of emissions for these diverse marine activities. This knowledge gap limits both our basic understanding of marine vessel emissions, and the data granularity that is needed to underpin policy or private sector interventions aimed at reducing emissions.

The currently accepted state-of-the-art method for estimating marine vessel emissions relies on a bottom-up model that uses vessel tracking data from the Automatic Identification System (AIS), which combines information on the vessel movement  with characteristics of each vessel including main and auxiliary engine powers, boiler power, as well as pollutant-specific emission factors that specify the kg of emissions per metric tonne of fuel used. These emissions factors vary for each pollutant by vessel type, vessel size, engine type, and fuel type \cite{olmer2017greenhouse, fourth2020greenhouse}. The IMO has validated this bottom-up approach against multiple independent data sources including: high-frequency continuous monitoring sensor data installed directly on vessels; vessel-level emissions data from the EU Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification emissions database; and other global emissions inventories \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. The IMO has also shown this bottom-up approach to be more reliable and substantially more granular than top-down approaches based solely on aggregate fuel consumption data \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. Consequently, the bottom-up approach has been widely adopted by the scientific community (a Google Scholar search in May 2025 for papers using the search term 'Automatic Identification System' 'emissions' yielded 6,290 results). 

This AIS-based method, however, still faces two fundamental challenges: 1) AIS coverage is incomplete and not fully representative of global maritime vessel activity; 2) key technical vessel characteristics necessary for bottom-up emissions modeling are often unknown, particularly for low-information vessels that are not listed in publicly available registries \cite{chen2024analysis}. First, AIS data only captures a fraction of vessel activity. Many vessels are not equipped with AIS systems or are not required to broadcast AIS \cite{taconet2019global}. Satellite imagery analysis has shown that about 75\% of industrial fishing vessels are not trackable by AIS, while over 25\% of vessels associated with transport (e.g., cargo, passenger) and offshore energy infrastructure (e.g. tankers, support vessels) are not tracked with AIS \cite{paolo2024satellite}. Even for vessels that do carry AIS, there can be gaps in data transmission due to challenges with the reception quality of satellites and terrestrial receivers, or due to intentional AIS disabling that may be associated with illegal activities or with legal activities such as hiding vessel locations in areas prone to piracy \cite{welch2022hot}. Changes in AIS adoption rate and the evolution of AIS technology over time also make it challenging to assess the trends of vessel activities using AIS data alone. For example, the introduction of Dynamic-AIS starting in 2022 has increased the AIS reception quality in some high traffic regions \cite{spire_documentation_2022}, resulting in significant increases in the vessel activity captured by AIS that do not necessarily reflect the trend in the actual amount of the activities (Figs. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}, \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type-top-flags}, \ref{fig:fig-co2-emissions-change-map-by-receiver-type}). The landscape of AIS data providers (Orbcomm, Spire Global, Exact Earth, Marine Traffic), and the type of AIS data each provides (satellite-based, terrestrial, dynamic) has also changed over time, further confounding the ability to study trends based on AIS data alone.

Here we develop a method to estimate high-resolution marine vessel emissions globally from 2017 through 2024 by fusing AIS data from broadcasting vessels with Sentinel-1 (S1) Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) detections of non-broadcasting vessels. For over 880,000 AIS-broadcasting vessels, we quantify emissions for each vessel using precise latitude/longitude/timestamp information for over 94 billion individual AIS messages, which are broadcast at frequencies of minutes to hours. We do so using previously published and validated methods for the bottom-up emissions model itself \cite{olmer2017greenhouse, fourth2020greenhouse}, while enriching key vessel characteristics metadata for low-information vessels using machine learning models (main engine power, design speed, and vessel type). For non-broadcasting vessels, we leverage over 29 million vessel detections from across more than 845,000 S1 scenes to quantify emissions at a monthly gridded 1x1-degree resolution and disaggregated by vessel size (across 10 length deciles) and vessel type (fishing or non-fishing). Our method leverages computing infrastructure and technical expertise developed by Global Fishing Watch \cite{gfw_website} 
 in processing AIS data \cite{kroodsma2018tracking} and S1 data \cite{paolo2024satellite}, and in estimating key vessel characteristics for vessels lacking readily available public information \cite{park2023tracking}. Our method, therefore, effectively addresses the two major challenges currently present in remotely measuring marine vessel emissions.

Our approach yields monthly, spatially explicit estimates of marine vessel emissions for three GHGs that drive climate change (carbon dioxide: CO$_{2}$, methane: CH$_{4}$, and nitrous oxide: N$_{2}$O) and six other non-GHG air pollutants that impact ambient air quality and health (carbon monoxide: CO, nitrogen oxides: NO$_{x}$, sulfur oxides: SO$_{x}$, particulate matter 2.5: PM$_{2.5}$, particulate matter: 10 PM$_{10}$, and volatile organic compounds: VOC$_{s}$). Focusing our main results on CO$_{2}$ as the primary greenhouse gas (with other GHGs and non-GHG air pollutants described in the supplement), we report trends in emissions from 2017 onwards, and compare trajectories of emission estimates based on either the AIS data alone or the fused AIS-S1 data. We compare the trend in marine vessel and terrestrial transportation CO$_{2}$ emissions over the same period. Our data also allows detailed mapping of emissions from vessel activities at a global scale with a breakdown across vessel classes, as well as across vessel activities (port stays; domestic trips; and international trips). By comparing emission trends from AIS-only models and our fused AIS–SAR approach, we reveal the true magnitude and evolving dynamics of the marine sector’s contribution to global and regional emissions.

\section{Results}

To correctly estimate both the magnitude and trends of marine emissions, data fusion of AIS and Sentinel-1 is essential. If only considering emissions from vessels that broadcast AIS, in line with currently accepted state-of-the-art models, global CO$_{2}$ emissions in 2024 would be 1.1 billion MT (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}A), and the rate of change from 2017 to 2024 would show a 34\% increase (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}B). However, when including non-broadcasting vessels using S1 detections, global CO$_{2}$ emissions in 2024 were higher at 1.3  billion MT (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}A), and the trend from 2017 to 2024 is more modest at a 22\% increase (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}B). Thus using only AIS data from broadcasting vessels \textit{underestimates} current global CO$_{2}$ emissions by 9\%, and \textit{overestimates} the increasing trend for 2017--2024 by 20\% (see Tables \ref{tab:total_percent_change_by_fleet} and \ref{tab:pollutant_ais_underestimation_overestimation_summary} for summaries of all other GHGs and pollutants). Fusing AIS and S1 data also provides a comprehensive picture of spatial marine vessel emissions patterns and how those patterns change over time (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}C and Fig. \ref{fig:fig-pollutant-maps}). There spatial trends in how emissions have changed over time in major shipping lanes.

Estimating emissions from non-broadcasting vessels not only offers new insights on the overall magnitude and temporal trends of global emissions, but provides nuanced spatial information and information disaggregated by fishing and non-fishing vessels. Across the entire 2017 to 2024 time series, 87.9\% of non-broadcasting emissions occurred inside the S1 footprint and in pixel-months that were imaged; 11.6\% occurred inside the S1 footprint but in pixel-months that weren't imaged; and 0.6\% occurred outside the S1 footprint entirely. Therefore, 87.9\% of our estimated non-broadcasting emissions are modeled purely using our emissions regression model (traditional R$^{2}$ = 0.94 inside the S1 footprint) (Table \ref{tab:all_performance_metrics_table}). 11.6\%  of our estimated non-broadcasting emissions are modeled using the detections classification model (F1 Score = 0.67 inside the S1 footprint), the detections regression model (traditional R$^{2}$ =0.76 inside the S1 footprint), and the emissions regression model (traditional R$^{2}$ = 0.94 inside the S1 footprint) (Table \ref{tab:all_performance_metrics_table}). In 2024, 26\% of CO$_{2}$ emissions from fishing vessels were from non-broadcasting vessels, and 7.3\% of CO$_{2}$ emissions from non-fishing vessels were from non-broadcasting vessels. For both types of vessels, this fraction of emissions from non-broadcasting vessels has been declining steadily since 2020. 

Our novel methods for quantifying key vessel characteristics that are necessary for bottom-up emissions modeling allow us to accurately estimate vessel-level AIS-based emissions. Using the main engine power and design speed values that are derived from our machine learning models, we achieve an out-of-sample model performance of 0.82 traditional R$^{2}$ when comparing our daily vessel-specific CO$_{2}$ emissions estimates against those derived from a proprietary vessel registry dataset of over 15,000 vessel-specific fuel consumption values that were measured during at-sea trials (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-registered-data-performance}). This can be compared to a value of 0.74 traditional R$^{2}$ which was obtained when instead using vessel characteristics from the lookup tables in \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}, which provides average main engine power and design speed for different vessel classes and sizes. Our approach therefore provides a performance improvement over the publicly available vessel characteristic data currently available in \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. We also measured the performance of our model's ability to estimate annual vessel-level CO$_{2}$ emissions estimates by comparing our estimates to those available in the publicly available European Maritime Safety Agency’s Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) database. Using a set of over 2,000 matched vessel-specific annual emissions estimates, we found a performance value of 0.81 traditional R$^{2}$ (Table \ref{tab:tab-performance-tolerance-table}).

Our high spatiotemporal resolution data allow for a nuanced look at the landscape of marine vessel emissions. The time series data reveal strong seasonal trends and event-driven variations in marine GHG emissions. We see consistent dips during major holidays including Christmas, New Year’s Day, and Chinese New Year, as well as during the COVID-19 pandemic (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-data-richness}A and Fig. \ref{fig:fig-pollutant-time-series}). Emissions from AIS-broadcasting can also be aggregated across various vessel characteristics, such as their vessel type or flag. Chemical and oil tankers, some of which transport fossil fuels including crude oil, are themselves the largest emitters. Trawlers, which use an energy-intensive fishing technique in which nets are dragged through the water, are the highest-emitting type of fishing vessel (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-data-richness}B). Emissions from AIS-broadcasting vessels can be assigned to port stays and port-to-port voyages by analyzing the movement tracks of each individual vessel (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-data-richness}C). China, Japan, and the United States had the most CO$_{2}$ emissions during within-country domestic trips in 2024. China, Malaysia, and the United States had the most CO$_{2}$ emissions during international trips that started or ended in these countries in 2024 (where the emissions from each trip are partitioned equally to its departure and arrival country). China, United States, and Indonesia had the most CO$_{2}$ emissions during port stays (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-data-richness}C).

\section{Discussion}

A high resolution picture of marine vessel emissions is essential to assess the impact of human activities on  the global climate and local population's health, and for designing effective policy solutions. However, incomplete vessel tracking and technical data processing challenges have hindered progress. Our method overcomes these limitations by fusing two independent data sources and provides the first high-resolution view of the magnitude and trends of greenhouse gas emissions and of other air pollutants from the majority of industrial marine vessels.  These spatiotemporal data would allow for analysis of the GHG emissions that drive climate change, as well as of other air pollutants that impact local air quality and health. These analyses could inform the design and evaluation of interventions such as port regulations, fuel standards, carbon credits, and vessel speed reduction zones. They could also be used to quantify the spatial health impacts of air pollution related to shipping and fishing activity, along with the associated environmental justice implications.


Carbon markets could play a role in mitigating marine vessel emissions. The International Maritime Organization is calling for a global price on carbon for shipping emissions \cite{imo_carbon_price}. In recent years, the European Union Emissions Trading System has already been expanded to include marine transportation under the EU emissions cap, and to subject vessel activity to and from European Economic Area ports to a carbon price \cite{kotzampasakis2025maritime}. Our analysis shows that increasing emissions have largely been driven by an increase in the number of vessels (i.e., the extensive margin), not by increases in speed for those vessels (i.e., the intensive margin). This finding suggests that implementing a carbon market based on limiting emissions intensity (the amount of emissions per unit of activity or production) could be an effective strategy for mitigating marine vessel emissions, even with a growing number of vessels. Carbon markets that are based on carbon credits for emissions intensity rather than just on capping emissions, such as India's emerging carbon market \cite{gupta2023assessing}, allow for overall economic growth while still incentivizing more efficient activity. This is consistent with a review paper on current marine vessel emissions datasets, which concluded that measures aimed at increasing technical efficiency of ships could be an effective way to decouple emissions growth from global trade demand \cite{dengreview}.

How large is the climate externality associated with marine vessels? By multiplying the social cost of carbon (SCC, using 283 USD/MT \cite{moore2024synthesis}) with our estimated non-fishing vessel emissions for 2021 (1.2 billion MT CO$_{2}$ from cargo, tanker, etc vessel types), we estimate a global externality of 340 billion USD.  In other words, the present value cost of 2021 carbon emissions from the marine transportation sector was about 340 billion USD, which represents about 5\% of the total value of all goods transported by maritime trade (using 6.63 trillion USD as the value of maritime-traded goods \cite{hoffmeister2022developing}). Using a similar approach, our voyage-level emissions dataset could be used to estimate the SCC for country-to-country trade flows, allowing for the calculation of country-to-country metrics such as the SCC per USD value of goods traded, or the SCC per metric tonne of goods traded. In principle, for vessels tracked with AIS, it is also possible to assign an externality cost to individual port-to-port trips. 

Our approach provides an example of how to resolve a more general challenge in environmental monitoring: the temporal trend in an environmental variable is often confounded by changes in monitoring itself. For marine vessel emissions, this challenge manifests as an increase in adoption of AIS and number of vessels being tracked over time, which lead current methods to overestimate the rate of growth of at-sea emissions (by 20\%). Similarly, more extensive monitoring of other environmental variables could lead us to overestimate their growth rates (for example, for invasive species discoveries \cite{costello2003pattern}).  Our approach fusing independent data sources is more robust to changes in monitoring over time than methods relying on a sole data source, and could be applied in other settings to produce unbiased estimates of environmental change.

\section*{Materials and Methods}

This work relies on multiple sources of spatial and tabular data to assess marine vessel emissions globally. We use AIS data, vessel registry information, and Sentinel-1 SAR derived data to construct our AIS-based emission model and the non-broadcasting emissions model (Fig. \ref{fig:gfw_emissions_framework_flowchart}).

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/gfw_emissions_framework_flowchart.png}

The following sections describe our approaches for AIS-broadcasting vessels and non-broadcasting vessels.

\subsection{AIS-broadcasting vessels}

\paragraph{AIS data description}

We use individual AIS messages (pings) from the latest version of GFW’s AIS pipeline v3 \cite{gfw_version_3}, which parses, cleans, and augments raw AIS data \cite{kroodsma2018tracking}.  This dataset also classifies whether a fishing vessel is actively engaged in fishing activities at the ping level. Additionally, we leverage GFW's algorithm for detecting port visits \cite{gfw_anchorages_website}, allowing us to aggregate ping-level emissions to the vessel-specific port stays, as well as to vessel-specific port-to-port voyages.

\paragraph{Vessel characteristics data}

We use vessel characteristics data from GFW's vessel metadata tables. For each vessel, this includes: (1) official registry information when available (Park et al., 2023), or (2) when registry data are unavailable, algorithm-derived vessel characteristics including vessel class, main engine power, maximum design speed, and gross tonnage—(Kroodsma et al., 2018). 

For vessels classified as "cargo", we implement a random forest classification sub-model that builds off the general vessel classification algorithm to differentiate cargo vessel sub-categories that align with IMO cargo vessel class types. The model is trained on port visit patterns from over 26,000 vessels with known IMO cargo vessel class types. Sequences of port visits are converted into usable model features using a Word2Vec model. Additional model features include vessel activity metrics such as port hours, average distance from shore, and average speed. With this approach, we can differentiate vessels that were previously grouped as an undifferentiated cargo vessel type into specific categories, including bulk carrier, container, general, refrigerated, and ro-ro (roll-on/roll-off for wheeled vehicle transport). The model achieves an F1 weighted average performance of 0.91. For vessels classified as "tanker" by the general vessel classification algorithm, we develop a another sub-model using a similar approach to differentiate tankers into chemical or oil, liquefied gas, and other liquids. This model achieves an F1 weighted average performance of 0.95 These refined cargo and vessel class categories allows us to more accurately assign auxiliary engine power and boiler power to each vessel, which are based on lookup tables according to vessel class and size \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. 

For main engine power, gross tonnage, and maximum design speed, we develop separate random forest regression models. These regression models were trained using prior vessel classification scores supplemented by activity and AIS messages data as additional features. All models were trained on datasets of around 360,000 labeled registry samples, with a holdout test set of 90,000 for an 80/20 train-test split. The main engine power and gross tonnage regression models achieved $R^2$ values exceeding 0.88 and 0.83, respectively.

The resulting vessel characteristics dataset contains the necessary metadata for estimating emissions using a bottom-up methodology for over 900,000 active AIS-broadcasting vessels.

\subsection{Emissions model for AIS-broadcasting vessels}

We adapted our AIS-based model from the methodologies described in the 2017 ICCT study on global shipping emissions \cite{olmer2017greenhouse} and the 2020 IMO Fourth Greenhouse Gas Study \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}, making modifications to better align with the structure and characteristics of our data sets. From a high-level, we: 1) determine the main engine energy use, auxiliary engine energy use, and boiler energy use for each individual AIS message based on dynamic vessel movement and static vessel characteristic data; 2) multiply the engine and boiler energy use numbers by pollutant- and engine-type-specific emissions factors in order to determine the amount of emissions for six pollutants associated with the engines and boiler for each ping; 3) add up the emissions for these six pollutants across the engines and boiler, to obtain the total emissions for each ping and each of these six pollutants; 4) estimate emissions for three additional pollutants that do not detailed information on emissions factors by simply multiplying the total CO$_{2}$ emissions for each ping by a conversion factor \cite{mayesshipping}.

Main engine energy use is estimated based on the number of hours between with each AIS ping and the previous ping, the main engine power from the vessel characteristics dataset, and a load factor used to adjust the estimate. The load factor is the product of four coefficients: (1) a weather correction factor (represented by distance to shore, a proxy that can be observed from AIS data - vessel operating byond 5 nautical miles from shore receive a value of 1.15; vessels operating within 5 nautical miles receive a value of 1.1 \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}); (2) a hull fouling correction factor, which causes resistance in the water (set at 1.07 according to \cite{olmer2017greenhouse}); and (3) a vessel draft correction factor (set at 0.85, the emissions-weighted average draft correction factor from \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}; and (4) a cubic term of vessel speed divided by  the vessel's maximum design speed. To determine the vessel speed associated with each AIS message, we use either: 1) the transmitted instantaneous speed associated with the AIS message if less than an hour has passed since the last message was transmitted; or 2) the implied speed for messages for which more than an hour has passed since the previous message (calculated as the distance traveled divided by the elapsed time between the two messages). For fishing vessels, we adjust the main engine load factor so that it falls between 0.2 and 0.9, and assign a main engine load factor of 0.75 to trawlers and dredge fishing vessels that are actively fishing due to the energy intensive nature of their fishing operations \cite{coello2015ais, sala2018economics}. 

In accordance with \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}, our model also accounts for differences in auxiliary engine power energy use and boiler energy use depending on the vessel’s operational phase—whether it is at berth, at anchor, maneuvering, or at sea. These phases are identified using AIS speed, distance to port, and distance to the coast. Power outputs for auxiliary engines and boilers, based on vessel class and size, are extracted from \cite{fourth2020greenhouse} and are applied accordingly for each phase.

After calculating energy use, we apply pollutant-specific emission factors to the main engine, auxiliary engine, and boiler for six GHGs and pollutants (CO$_{2}$, CH$_{4}$, CO, NO$_{x}$, SO$_{x}$, N$_{2}$O), following \cite{olmer2017greenhouse}. These factors are each averaged from across the relevant pollutant factors associated with SSD, MSD, and HSD engine types, which together represent approximately 98\% of vessels engines \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. To get total emissions for each AIS message (i.e., emissions from the previous ping in a segment to the current one), we sum emissions from both engines and boilers. For VOCs, PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$, which do not have as detailed information on emissions factors, we instead multiply the CO$_{2}$ emissions by pollutant-specific conversion factors \cite{mayesshipping}. We also apply main engine low-load correction factors since engines operating at loads below 20\% operate inefficiently and emit more of certain pollutants based on how low the engine is operating (CH$_{4}$, CO, N$_{2}$O, NO$_{x}$, VOCs, PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$). We apply these correction factor on the main engine emissions for all AIS message with a main engine load below 20\%.

Starting January 1, 2020, the IMO mandated a low sulfur fuel switch, reducing the allowable sulfur content from 3.5\% to 0.5\% \cite{imo_sulphur_2020}. To account for this, for data starting on January 1, 2020 we apply a correction factor to SO$_{x}$, as well as PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$ which are partially produced by sulfur. The SO$_{x}$ emissions factor scales linearly with fuel sulfur percentage content \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. For pre-2020 data, we use an emissions factor based on a global average sulfur content of 2.5\% \cite{olmer2017greenhouse}. Assuming the sulfur content drops from 2.5\% to 0.5\% starting in 2020, this would correspond to an 80\% drop in sulfur content and an 80\% drop in the sulfur emissions factor. This 80\% is consistent with several studies that looked the the impact this new requirement had on global sulfur emissions (\cite{yuan2024abrupt, @yoshioka2024warming}). PM emissions factors are based on an equation that is non-linear to sulfur fuel content \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. Using this equation and again assuming an 80\% drop in sulfur content we estimate a 79.6\% drop in the PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$ emissions factors. We apply these adjusted emissions factors for all SO$_{x}$, PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$ emissions starting in January 1, 2020.


\subsection{Validating AIS-broadcasting emissions model}

We use two distinct emissions datasets for validation, one to assess the impact of updated vessel metadata from our random forest models on performance, and another to evaluate the model’s overall performance using emissions associated with actual vessel activity.

\subsubsection{Registered data validation}

We have access to a confidential registered dataset of vessels' main engine fuel consumption measured during sea trials over a 24-hour period at a given speed, along with each vessel’s main engine power and design speed. Because these consumption values are not associated with specific trips that can be matched to our trip-level emissions estimates, direct comparison of derived emissions is not possible. However, this dataset allows us to validate the emissions generated by the main engine model module and assess the impact of updated metadata from our random forest models. Specifically, we compare the performance of the main engine model emissions estimates using main engine power and design speed values from the original IMO lookup table \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}  compared with those obtained when using our vessel-specific random forest model predictions of main engine power and design speed. Furthermore, we define the performance ceiling achievable through improved vessel metadata by testing the model with the actual engine power and design speed recorded in the registered dataset.

For a validation sample of 15,679 vessels, we ran the main engine model to calculate main engine energy use, incorporating the weather correction factor for offshore navigation along with the other default hull-fouling and draft correction factors from our main model, over a 24-hour period at the consumption speed provided in the registered dataset. We test three different sets of vessel metadata. For each set, main engine power and design speed were taken from either: (1) Table 81 lookup values from \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}; (2) our vessel-specific random forest model predictions; or (3) the registered dataset. Main engine energy use was then converted to emissions using the same CO$_{2}$ emission factor as in our main model. Since the registered dataset provides vessel-specific main fuel consumption values and not actual emissions values, we converted the registered fuel consumption to emissions by applying a fuel-to-CO$_{2}$ conversion factor of 3.12 (kg CO$_{2}$/tonne fuel), derived from the heavy fuel oil (HFO) and marine diesel oil (MDO) factors weighted by their contribution to global fuel consumption, as reported in \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. This finally allows us to assess the performance of our model-estimated CO$_{2}$ emissions to those from the registered dataset.

This comparison shows that the updated vessel metadata using random forest models substantially improves model performance compared to that achieved using the original \cite{fourth2020greenhouse} vessel metadata, bringing the results closer to the model performance ceiling.

\subsection{Decomposition analysis}

In order to better understand the drivers of emissions trends, we perform a decomposition analysis to assess the relative contributions of total vessel kilowatt-hours and average vessel speed. To do so, we first construct a dataset of annual total hours, total kW-hours, and total distance traveled, aggregated across both the AIS-broadcasting and non-broadcasting vessels, and disaggregated by vessel type (fishing and non-fishing), and decile length bins, for 2017-2024. To build this dataset, for AIS-broadcasting vessels we simply use the AIS-based emissions estimates as described above for the main analysis. For non-broadcasting vessels, we extrapolate total hours, total kW-hours, and total distance traveled in the exact same way as we extrapolate emissions for the main analysis. We do so by simply multiplying AIS-based total hours, total kW-hours, and total distance traveled by the ratio of non-broadcasting to AIS-broadcasting vessels for each pixel, month, vessel type, and vessel decile bin. We then aggregate the monthly non-broadcasting hours, kW-hours, and distance traveled to an annual time scale, and then add these values to the corresponding AIS-broadcasting values to get total numbers aggregated across both AIS-broadcasting and non-broadcasting vessels. Finally, we calculate average speed for each year (in knots), vessel type, and decile length bin by dividing the corresponding total distance traveled (in nautical miles) by the total hours. For each year, vessel type, and decile length bin we also take the total CO$_{2}$ emissions as estimated in the main analysis.

With this dataset, we next train and test a random forest regression model to predict CO$_{2}$ emissions for each  year, vessel type, and decile length bin as a function of total kW-hours, average speed, vessel type, and decile length bin as a categorical feature. We do so using the the \emph{tidymodels} suite of R machine learning packages \cite{tidymodels}. We first split the dataset into a training dataset (using 80\% of the observations) and a holdout testing dataset (using 20\% of the observations). Using the training dataset, we use the the \emph{ranger} R package \cite{ranger} to train a random forest model \cite{breiman2001random} with 1000 trees, a minimum node size of 5, and an $mtry$ value of 2 (the minimum number of variables used to split each node; we chose this as the square root of the number of model features, which is 4). With the trained model, we then test the model performance using the testing dataset by comparing the predicted annual CO$_{2}$ emissions to the actual CO$_{2}$ emissions. We do so by using a traditional $R^2$ metric which uses sum of squares and allows for negative values (as opposed to using an $R^2$ value based on correlation, which forces a value between 0 and 1) \cite{yardstick}. This yields a more conservation estimate of performance. Finally, with the model performance now estimated, we train the final model using the entire dataset (combining both the training dataset and testing dataset). This final model is then used for scenario predictions.

Using the final trained model, we make predictions for four different scenarios:

1. Observed kW-hours and observed speed. Here we simply use the dataset as-is, and make predictions for CO$_{2}$. This scenario corresponds to the world as we have observed it.

2. Observed kW-hours and constant speed. Here we use the observed kW-hours as-is, but set the speed at constant values corresponding to what they were in 2017 (by vessel class and length bin decile).

3. Constant kW-hours and observed speed. Here we use the observed speed as-is, but set the kW-hours at constant values corresponding to what they were in 2017 (by vessel class and length bin decile).

4. Constant kW-hours and constant speed. Here we set both the kW-hours and speed at constant values corresponding to what they were in 2017 (by vessel class and length bin decile).

In this way, (1) reconstructs predictions for our observed data. On the other end of the spectrum, (4) constructs a scenario for what the world would have looked like if neither kW-hours or speed had changed over time. The difference between predictions from (2) and (4) isolate the impact of changing kW-hours over time. Finally, the difference in predictions from (3) and (4) isolates the impact of changing speed over time.

\subsection{Disentangling trends in AIS-based emissions data: the introduction of Dynamic AIS}

AIS messages are broadcast from vessels and then received through one of three technologies: satellite-based receivers, terrestrial receivers, and dynamic receivers. Satellite and terrestrial receivers are the oldest receiver types, while 2022 saw the widespread adoption of 'Dynamic-AIS' in several regions throughout the world. Dynamic AIS is a technology that is implemented by Spire, one of the major AIS data providers, in which satellite-enabled AIS receivers are installed on vessels that travel through some of the world's busiest shipping lanes  such as the South China Sea \cite{spire_documentation_2022}. Through GFW, we can determine the receiver type for every single AIS message in the dataset over time. In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}, we summarize the amount of estimated emissions and the number of distinct vessels that were detected by the three different types of AIS receivers. In 2022, we can clearly see a sharp rise in the amount of emissions that were estimated from Dynamic AIS, which intuitively coincides with a decrease in the emissions that are estimated from satellite and terrestrial receivers. On aggregate, this led to an increase in total estimated emissions. The number of distinct vessels detected by dynamic AIS also sharply rose in 2022, almost equaling the number of vessels detected by all receiver types.

In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type-top-flags}, we see the amount of quantified emissions for the top 10 vessel flags broken apart by AIS receiver type. The trends are similar to those seen on aggregate ( Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}), although there is variation in terms of the increase in Dynamic AIS detections across different flags. This is likely driven by whether or not each flag travels through regions where Dynamic AIS was widely adopted. It is important to note that towards the end of 2021, China experience a blackout of terrestrial AIS signals due to new privacy laws that were being implemented \cite{benefits_dybnamic_ais}. This sharp decline from 2021 to 2022 in terrestrial-based emissions is clearly evident for China. Without the introduction of Dynamic AIS, it is possible that this disruption may have resulted in erroneously small emissions in China following the terrestrial receiver blackout. This is another example of how multiple complementary technologies can be fused in order to maximize emissions coverage over time.

Dynamic AIS was mostly implemented in busy shipping lanes, with clear concentration in areas including the South China Sea, Europe, and the Gulf of Mexico. These regions also generally correspond to decreasing use of satellite and terrestrial receivers. Overall, the largest total change in emissions is generally concentrated in the same regions that saw the largest uptake of Dynamic AIS.
