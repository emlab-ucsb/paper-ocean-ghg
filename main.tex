\begin{abstract}

 Quantifying environmental trends is a fundamental aspect of assessing and managing environmental impacts. This requires a monitoring scheme for measuring changes over time. As technology improves, monitoring itself may also change over time, making it challenging to disentangle actual environmental changes from changes in monitoring. As improved technology allows for more and more comprehensive monitoring over time, this may simultaneously lead to overestimates in the increasing trends of environmental problems and underestimates of their overall scale. Combining multiple monitoring data sources has the potential to reduce these biases. Here we develop a novel method for quantifying high spatiotemporal resolution global ocean-based greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by combining two unique satellite-based monitoring sources: 1) Automatic Identification System (AIS) to estimate vessel-level emissions for the over 660,000 boats that broadcast their positions using AIS; and 2) synthetic aperture radar (SAR) data from the Sentinel-1 satellite constellation to estimate emissions for boats that do not use AIS. When only looking at emissions based on AIS, global emissions in 2023 were 1,100 million metric tonnes (MMT), and trend from 2016 to 2023 shows a 33\% increase. When also adding in non-broadcasting vessels using Sentinel-1 detections, global emissions in 2023 were 1,320 MMT, and the trend from 2016 to 2023 is more modest at only a 21\% increase. This suggests that if only using AIS data, global emissions in 2023 would be underestimated by 16\%, and the increasing trend from 2016 to 2023 would be overestimated by 57\%. Our approach therefore not only provides a robust method for quantifying comprehensive high-resolution spatiotemporal emissions data that could be used for a variety of interventions for mitigating emissions, but also provides an example of how multiple data sources can be used to disentangle actual environmental changes from improved monitoring and more accurately quantify both the scale and trends of environmental problems.

\end{abstract}

The total operational hours of these vessels increase 104\% over this time period, while the total main engine power for these vessels saw a 37\% increase. This provides some evidence that a primary driver of increasing observed emissions at a global scale (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-emissions-by-fleet}) could be the increasing number of vessels that broadcast AIS.

For example, for an earliest operation year of 2015, we can see how CO2 emissions change over time for just those vessels that have been operating since 2015. Regardless of which year we take as a baseline, the emissions for the fleet from any given year shows a consistent decreasing pattern from the first year to the last year. This is also seen in the number of broadcasting vessels, operating hours, and total main engine power. This provides further evidence that the main source of increasing observed emissions seen across all vessels at a global scale (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-emissions-by-fleet}) is primarily driven by the increasing number of vessels that broadcast AIS over time (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-total-annual-unique-vessels-hours-power}).
