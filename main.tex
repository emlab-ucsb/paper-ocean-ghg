\begin{abstract} \bfseries \boldmath

 Increased concentrations of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere due to human activities trap heat and cause the planet to warm, leading to changes in the global climate. The transportation sector contributes significantly towards these emissions through ocean-based vessel emissions as well as aviation, railways, and roadways. Yet despite advances in marine vessel tracking technology and emission inventories, a substantial and unquantified fraction of vessel activity remains invisible in current global datasets.  As a result, global inventories chronically underestimate marine vessel emissions and misrepresent trends, undermining efforts to monitor, regulate, and mitigate their climate impacts. We developed a method for quantifying marine vessel emissions at global scale by fusing two independent types of satellite data: vessel tracking using the Automatic Identification System (AIS) to estimate vessel-level emissions, and vessel detection using synthetic aperture radar (SAR) imagery to estimate emissions for vessels that do not use AIS. We found that vessels emitted approximately 1.3 billion metric tonnes of CO$_{2}$ in 2024, accounting for 15\% of global fossil fuel emissions from the transportation sector. We also found that marine vessel CO$_{2}$ emissions increased by 31\% between 2017 and 2024, nine times the increase seen in the rest of the transportation sector. Because many vessels do not broadcast AIS, current approaches solely based on AIS \textit{underestimate} the magnitude of 2024 emissions by 9\%. Meanwhile, because AIS coverage has generally been increasing over time, current global estimates \textit{overestimate} the increasing emissions trend by 22\% (from 2017 to 2024). Our approach provides a robust estimate of the magnitude, trends, and spatial patterns of marine vessel GHG emissions and non-GHG air pollutant emissions. This information can serve as a key input to designing and evaluating interventions aimed at mitigating climate change.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The transportation sector is a major source of global greenhouse gas (GHG) and non-GHG air pollution emissions, responsible for 21\% of all CO$_{2}$ emissions globally in 2024 \cite{JRC143227}. While estimation of emissions is well understood in some transportation sectors (e.g., railways, aviation, and roadways), fundamental gaps remain in the estimation of marine vessel emissions. These emissions come primarily from the transportation of goods via shipping vessels, carriage of passengers, and fishing activity, with a smaller contribution from other industrial activities such as dredging and offshore infrastructure maintenance. Because these diverse vessel types vary dramatically in the extent to which vessel behavior is tracked and vessel characteristics are understood, we lack a comprehensive estimate of emissions for these diverse marine activities. This knowledge gap limits both our basic understanding of marine vessel emissions, and the data granularity that is needed to underpin policy or private sector interventions aimed at reducing emissions.

The currently accepted state-of-the-art method for estimating marine vessel emissions relies on a bottom-up model that uses vessel tracking data from the Automatic Identification System (AIS), which combines information on the vessel movement  with characteristics of each vessel including main and auxiliary engine powers, boiler power, as well as pollutant-specific emission factors that specify the kg of emissions per metric tonne of fuel used. These emissions factors vary for each pollutant by vessel type, vessel size, engine type, and fuel type \cite{olmer2017greenhouse, fourth2020greenhouse}. The IMO has validated this bottom-up approach against multiple independent data sources including: high-frequency continuous monitoring sensor data installed directly on vessels; vessel-level emissions data from the EU Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification emissions database; and other global emissions inventories \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. The IMO has also shown this bottom-up approach to be more reliable and substantially more granular than top-down approaches based solely on aggregate fuel consumption data \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. Consequently, the bottom-up approach has been widely adopted by the scientific community (a Google Scholar search in January 2026 for articles using the search term ``Automatic Identification System'' ``emissions'' yielded 6,880 results). 

This AIS-based method, however, still faces two fundamental challenges: 1) AIS coverage is incomplete and not fully representative of global maritime vessel activity; 2) key technical vessel characteristics necessary for bottom-up emissions modeling are often unknown, particularly for low-information vessels that are not listed in publicly available registries \cite{chen2024analysis}. First, AIS data only captures a fraction of vessel activity. Many vessels are not equipped with AIS systems or are not required to broadcast AIS \cite{taconet2019global}. Satellite imagery analysis has shown that about 75\% of industrial fishing vessels are not trackable by AIS, while over 25\% of vessels associated with transport (e.g., cargo, passenger) and offshore energy infrastructure (e.g. tankers, support vessels) are not tracked with AIS \cite{paolo2024satellite}. Even for vessels that do carry AIS, there can be gaps in data transmission due to challenges with the reception quality of satellites and terrestrial receivers, or due to intentional AIS disabling that may be associated with illegal activities or with legal activities such as hiding vessel locations in areas prone to piracy \cite{welch2022hot}. Changes in AIS adoption rate and the evolution of AIS technology over time also make it challenging to assess the trends of vessel activities using AIS data alone. For example, the introduction of Dynamic-AIS starting in 2022 has increased the AIS reception quality in some high traffic regions \cite{spire_documentation_2022}, resulting in significant increases in the vessel activity captured by AIS that do not necessarily reflect the trend in the actual amount of the activities (Figs. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}, \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type-top-flags}, \ref{fig:fig-co2-emissions-change-map-by-receiver-type}). The landscape of AIS data providers (Orbcomm, Spire Global, Exact Earth, Marine Traffic), and the type of AIS data each provides (satellite-based, terrestrial, dynamic) has also changed over time, further confounding the ability to study trends based on AIS data alone.

Here we develop a method to estimate high-resolution marine vessel emissions globally from 2017 through 2024 by fusing AIS data from broadcasting vessels with Sentinel-1 (S1) Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) detections of non-broadcasting vessels. For over 880,000 AIS-broadcasting vessels, we quantify emissions for each vessel using precise latitude/longitude/timestamp information for over 94 billion individual AIS messages, which are broadcast at frequencies of minutes to hours. We do so using previously published and validated methods for the bottom-up emissions model itself \cite{olmer2017greenhouse, fourth2020greenhouse}, while enriching key vessel characteristics metadata for low-information vessels using machine learning models (main engine power, design speed, and vessel type). For emissions from non-broadcasting vessels (also sometimes known as 'dark vessels' \cite{rowlands2019satellite}), we leverage over 29 million vessel detections from across more than 845,000 S1 scenes to quantify emissions at a monthly gridded 1x1-degree resolution and disaggregated by vessel type (fishing or non-fishing). Our method leverages computing infrastructure and technical expertise developed by Global Fishing Watch \cite{gfw_website} 
 in processing AIS data \cite{kroodsma2018tracking} and S1 data \cite{paolo2024satellite}, and in estimating key vessel characteristics for vessels lacking readily available public information \cite{park2023tracking}. Our method, therefore, effectively addresses the two major challenges currently present in remotely measuring marine vessel emissions.

Our approach yields monthly, spatially explicit estimates of marine vessel emissions for three GHGs that drive climate change (carbon dioxide: CO$_{2}$, methane: CH$_{4}$, and nitrous oxide: N$_{2}$O) and six other non-GHG air pollutants that impact ambient air quality and health (carbon monoxide: CO, nitrogen oxides: NO$_{x}$, sulfur oxides: SO$_{x}$, particulate matter 2.5: PM$_{2.5}$, particulate matter: 10 PM$_{10}$, and volatile organic compounds: VOC$_{s}$). Focusing our main results on CO$_{2}$ as the primary greenhouse gas (with other GHGs and non-GHG air pollutants described in the supplement), we report trends in emissions from 2017 onwards, and compare trajectories of emission estimates based on either the AIS data alone or the fused AIS-S1 data. We compare the trend in marine vessel CO$_{2}$ emissions to those from other transportation sectors over the same period. Our data also allows detailed mapping of emissions from vessel activities at a global scale with a breakdown across vessel classes, as well as across vessel activities (port stays; domestic trips; and international trips). By comparing emission trends from AIS-only models and our fused AIS–SAR approach, we reveal the true magnitude and evolving dynamics of the marine sector’s contribution to global and regional emissions.

\section{Results}

To correctly estimate both the magnitude and trends of marine emissions, data fusion of AIS and Sentinel-1 is essential. If only considering emissions from vessels that broadcast AIS, in line with currently accepted state-of-the-art models, global CO$_{2}$ emissions in 2024 would be 1.1 billion MT (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}A), and the rate of change from 2017 to 2024 would show a 34\% increase (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}B). However, when including non-broadcasting vessels using S1 detections, global CO$_{2}$ emissions in 2024 were higher at 1.3  billion MT (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}A), and the trend from 2017 to 2024 is more modest at a 22\% increase (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}B). Thus using only AIS data from broadcasting vessels \textit{underestimates} current global CO$_{2}$ emissions by 9\%, and \textit{overestimates} the increasing trend for 2017--2024 by 22\% (see Tables \ref{tab:total_percent_change_by_fleet} and \ref{tab:pollutant_ais_underestimation_overestimation_summary} for summaries of all other GHGs and pollutants). Fusing AIS and S1 data also provides a comprehensive picture of spatial marine vessel emissions patterns and how those patterns change over time (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-by-data-source-and-maps}C and Fig. \ref{fig:fig-pollutant-maps}).

Estimating emissions from non-broadcasting vessels not only offers new insights on the overall magnitude and temporal trends of global emissions, but also provides nuanced spatial information and information disaggregated by fishing and non-fishing vessels (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-spatial-temporal-richness-by-fleet-total-pseudolog}). Across the entire 2017 to 2024 time series, 86.4\% of non-broadcasting emissions occurred inside the S1 footprint and in pixel-months that were imaged; 12.5\% occurred inside the S1 footprint but in pixel-months that weren't imaged; and 1.1\% occurred outside the S1 footprint entirely (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-spatial-coverage-footprint}). Therefore, 86.4\% of our estimated non-broadcasting emissions are modeled purely using our emissions regression model (traditional R$^{2}$ = 0.94 inside the S1 footprint) (Table \ref{tab:all_performance_metrics_table}). 12.5\%  of our estimated non-broadcasting emissions are modeled using the detections classification model (F1 Score = 0.67 inside the S1 footprint), the detections regression model (traditional R$^{2}$ =0.76 inside the S1 footprint), and the emissions regression model (traditional R$^{2}$ = 0.94 inside the S1 footprint) (Table \ref{tab:all_performance_metrics_table}). In 2024, 25.4\% of CO$_{2}$ emissions from fishing vessels were from non-broadcasting vessels, and 7.4\% of CO$_{2}$ emissions from non-fishing vessels were from non-broadcasting vessels. For both types of vessels, this fraction of emissions from non-broadcasting vessels has been declining steadily since 2020.

Our novel methods for quantifying key vessel characteristics that are necessary for bottom-up emissions modeling allow us to accurately estimate vessel-level AIS-based emissions. Using the main engine power and design speed values that are derived from our machine learning models, we achieve an out-of-sample model performance of 0.82 traditional R$^{2}$ when comparing our daily vessel-specific CO$_{2}$ emissions estimates against those derived from a proprietary vessel registry dataset of over 15,000 vessel-specific fuel consumption values that were measured during at-sea trials (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-registered-data-performance}). This can be compared to a value of 0.74 traditional R$^{2}$ which was obtained when instead using vessel characteristics from the lookup tables in \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}, which provides average main engine power and design speed for different vessel classes and sizes. Our approach therefore provides a performance improvement over the publicly available vessel characteristic data currently available in \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. We also measured the performance of our model's ability to estimate annual vessel-level CO$_{2}$ emissions estimates by comparing our estimates to those available in the publicly available European Maritime Safety Agency’s Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) database. Using a set of over 2,000 matched vessel-specific annual emissions estimates, we found a performance value of 0.81 traditional R$^{2}$ (Table \ref{tab:tab-performance-tolerance-table}).

Our high spatiotemporal resolution data allow for a nuanced look at the landscape of marine vessel emissions. The time series data reveal strong seasonal trends and event-driven variations in marine GHG emissions. We see consistent dips during major holidays including Christmas, New Year’s Day, and Chinese New Year, as well as during the COVID-19 pandemic (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-spatial-temporal-richness-by-fleet-total-pseudolog}A, Fig. \ref{fig:fig-data-richness}A, and Fig. \ref{fig:fig-pollutant-time-series}). Emissions from AIS-broadcasting can also be aggregated across various vessel characteristics, such as their vessel type or flag. Chemical and oil tankers, some of which transport fossil fuels including crude oil, are themselves the largest emitters. Trawlers, which use an energy-intensive fishing technique in which nets are dragged through the water, are the highest-emitting type of fishing vessel (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-data-richness}B). Emissions from AIS-broadcasting vessels can be assigned to port stays and port-to-port voyages by analyzing the movement tracks of each individual vessel (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-data-richness}C). China, Japan, and the United States had the most CO$_{2}$ emissions during within-country domestic trips in 2024. China, Malaysia, and the United States had the most CO$_{2}$ emissions during international trips that started or ended in these countries in 2024 (where the emissions from each trip are partitioned equally to its departure and arrival country). China, United States, and Indonesia had the most CO$_{2}$ emissions during port stays (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-data-richness}C).

Marine vessel CO$_{2}$ emissions increased by 31\% between 2017 and 2024, nine times faster than the growth rate of emissions from all other transportation-related sectors including railways, road transportation, aviation, and pipelines (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-emissions-marine-other}B, \cite{JRC143227}).

\section{Discussion}

A high resolution picture of marine vessel emissions is essential to assess the impact of human activities on  the global climate and local population's health, and for designing effective policy solutions. However, incomplete vessel tracking and technical data processing challenges have hindered progress. Our method overcomes these limitations by fusing two independent data sources and provides the first high-resolution view of the magnitude and trends of greenhouse gas emissions and of other air pollutants from the majority of industrial marine vessels.  These spatiotemporal data would allow for analysis of the GHG emissions that drive climate change, as well as of other air pollutants that impact local air quality and health. These analyses could inform the design and evaluation of interventions such as port regulations, fuel standards, carbon credits, and vessel speed reduction zones. They could also be used to quantify the spatial health impacts of air pollution related to shipping and fishing activity, along with the associated environmental justice implications.


Carbon markets could play a role in mitigating marine vessel emissions. The International Maritime Organization is calling for a global price on carbon for shipping emissions \cite{imo_carbon_price}. In recent years, the European Union Emissions Trading System has already been expanded to include marine transportation under the EU emissions cap, and to subject vessel activity to and from European Economic Area ports to a carbon price \cite{kotzampasakis2025maritime}. Implementing a carbon market based on limiting emissions intensity (the amount of emissions per unit of activity or production) could be an effective strategy for mitigating marine vessel emissions, even with a growing number of vessels. Carbon markets that are based on carbon credits for emissions intensity rather than just on capping emissions, such as India's emerging carbon market \cite{gupta2023assessing}, allow for overall economic growth while still incentivizing more efficient activity. This is consistent with a review paper on current marine vessel emissions datasets, which concluded that measures aimed at increasing technical efficiency of ships could be an effective way to decouple emissions growth from global trade demand \cite{dengreview}.

How large is the climate externality social cost (SC) associated with marine transportation vessels? By multiplying the social cost of carbon (283 USD/MT \cite{moore2024synthesis}), social cost of methane (2,900 USD/MT \cite{wang2023damage}), and social cost of nitrous oxide (49,600 USD/MT \cite{wang2023damage}) each respectively with our estimated emissions for these pollutants from non-fishing vessels (e.g., cargo, tanker, etc.), we estimate a global climate externality of 314 billion USD in 2021. This represents about 5\% of the total value of all goods transported by maritime trade (using 6.63 trillion USD as the 2021 value of maritime-traded goods \cite{hoffmeister2022developing}). Using a similar approach, our voyage-level emissions dataset could be used to estimate the social cost climate externalities for country-to-country trade flows, allowing for the calculation of country-to-country metrics such as the SC per USD value of goods traded, or the SC per metric tonne of goods traded. In principle, for vessels tracked with AIS, it is also possible to assign an externality cost to individual port-to-port trips. Beyond climate externalities, marine transportation also causes significant local health impact externalities from air pollution \cite{zhang2021global}.

Our approach provides an example of how to resolve a more general challenge in environmental monitoring: the temporal trend in an environmental variable is often confounded by changes in monitoring itself. For marine vessel emissions, this challenge manifests as an increase in adoption of AIS and number of vessels being tracked over time, which lead current methods to overestimate the rate of growth of at-sea emissions (by 22\%). Similarly, more extensive monitoring of other environmental variables could lead us to overestimate their growth rates (for example, for invasive species discoveries \cite{costello2003pattern}).  Our approach fusing independent data sources is more robust to changes in monitoring over time than methods relying on a sole data source, and could be applied in other settings to produce unbiased estimates of environmental change.

\section*{Materials and Methods}

This work relies on multiple sources of spatial and tabular data to assess marine vessel emissions globally. We use AIS data, vessel registry information, Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 derived data to construct our AIS-based emission model and the non-broadcasting emissions model (Fig. \ref{fig:gfw_emissions_framework_flowchart}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig-framework-flowchart.png}
    \caption{Conceptual flowchart for estimating emissions using the AIS-based emissions model and the non-broadcasting emissions model.}
    \label{fig:gfw_emissions_framework_flowchart}
\end{figure}

The following sections describe our approaches for AIS-broadcasting vessels and non-broadcasting vessels.

\subsection{AIS-broadcasting vessels}

\subsubsection{AIS data description}

\paragraph{AIS messages, port visits, and voyages}

We use individual AIS messages (pings) from the latest version of GFW’s AIS pipeline v3 \cite{gfw_version_3}, which parses, cleans, and augments raw AIS data \cite{kroodsma2018tracking}.  This dataset also classifies whether a fishing vessel is actively engaged in fishing activities at the ping level. Additionally, we leverage GFW's algorithm for detecting port visits \cite{gfw_anchorages_website}, allowing us to aggregate ping-level emissions to the vessel-specific port stays, as well as to vessel-specific port-to-port voyages.

\paragraph{Vessel characteristics data}

We use vessel characteristics data from GFW's vessel metadata tables. For each vessel, this includes: (1) official registry information when available (Park et al., 2023), or (2) when registry data are unavailable, algorithm-derived vessel characteristics including vessel class, main engine power, maximum design speed, and gross tonnage—(Kroodsma et al., 2018). 

For vessels classified as "cargo", we implement a random forest classification sub-model that builds off the general vessel classification algorithm to differentiate cargo vessel sub-categories that align with IMO cargo vessel class types. The model is trained on port visit patterns from over 26,000 vessels with known IMO cargo vessel class types. Sequences of port visits are converted into usable model features using a Word2Vec model. Additional model features include vessel activity metrics such as port hours, average distance from shore, and average speed. With this approach, we can differentiate vessels that were previously grouped as an undifferentiated cargo vessel type into specific categories, including bulk carrier, container, general, refrigerated, and ro-ro (roll-on/roll-off for wheeled vehicle transport). The model achieves an F1 weighted average performance of 0.91. For vessels classified as "tanker" by the general vessel classification algorithm, we develop a another sub-model using a similar approach to differentiate tankers into chemical or oil, liquefied gas, and other liquids. This model achieves an F1 weighted average performance of 0.95 These refined cargo and vessel class categories allows us to more accurately assign auxiliary engine power and boiler power to each vessel, which are based on lookup tables according to vessel class and size \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. 

For main engine power, gross tonnage, and maximum design speed, we develop separate random forest regression models. These regression models were trained using prior vessel classification scores supplemented by activity and AIS messages data as additional features. All models were trained on datasets of around 360,000 labeled registry samples, with a holdout test set of 90,000 for an 80/20 train-test split. The main engine power and gross tonnage regression models achieved $R^2$ values exceeding 0.88 and 0.83, respectively.

The resulting vessel characteristics dataset contains the necessary metadata for estimating emissions using a bottom-up methodology for over 900,000 active AIS-broadcasting vessels.

\subsubsection{Emissions model for AIS-broadcasting vessels}

We adapted our AIS-based model from the methodologies described in the 2017 ICCT study on global shipping emissions \cite{olmer2017greenhouse} and the 2020 IMO Fourth Greenhouse Gas Study \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}, making modifications to better align with the structure and characteristics of our data sets. From a high-level, we: 1) determine the main engine energy use, auxiliary engine energy use, and boiler energy use for each individual AIS message based on dynamic vessel movement and static vessel characteristic data; 2) multiply the engine and boiler energy use numbers by pollutant- and engine-type-specific emissions factors in order to determine the amount of emissions for six pollutants associated with the engines and boiler for each ping; 3) add up the emissions for these six pollutants across the engines and boiler, to obtain the total emissions for each ping and each of these six pollutants; 4) estimate emissions for three additional pollutants that do not detailed information on emissions factors by simply multiplying the total CO$_{2}$ emissions for each ping by a conversion factor \cite{mayesshipping}.

Main engine energy use is estimated based on the number of hours between each AIS ping and the previous ping, the main engine power from the vessel characteristics dataset, and a load factor used to adjust the estimate. The load factor is the product of four coefficients: (1) a weather correction factor (represented by distance to shore, a proxy that can be observed from AIS data - vessel operating byond 5 nautical miles from shore receive a value of 1.15; vessels operating within 5 nautical miles receive a value of 1.1 \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}); (2) a hull fouling correction factor, which causes resistance in the water (set at 1.07 according to \cite{olmer2017greenhouse}); and (3) a vessel draft correction factor (set at 0.85, the emissions-weighted average draft correction factor from \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}; and (4) a cubic term of vessel speed divided by  the vessel's maximum design speed. To determine the vessel speed associated with each AIS message, we use either: 1) the transmitted instantaneous speed associated with the AIS message if less than an hour has passed since the last message was transmitted; or 2) the implied speed for messages for which more than an hour has passed since the previous message (calculated as the distance traveled divided by the elapsed time between the two messages). For fishing vessels, we adjust the main engine load factor so that it falls between 0.2 and 0.9, and assign a main engine load factor of 0.75 to trawlers and dredge fishing vessels that are actively fishing due to the energy intensive nature of their fishing operations \cite{coello2015ais, sala2018economics}. 

In accordance with \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}, our model also accounts for differences in auxiliary engine power energy use and boiler energy use depending on the vessel’s operational phase—whether it is at berth, at anchor, maneuvering, or at sea. These phases are identified using AIS speed, distance to port, and distance to the coast. Power outputs for auxiliary engines and boilers, based on vessel class and size, are extracted from \cite{fourth2020greenhouse} and are applied accordingly for each phase.

After calculating energy use, we apply pollutant-specific emission factors to the main engine, auxiliary engine, and boiler for six GHGs and pollutants (CO$_{2}$, CH$_{4}$, CO, NO$_{x}$, SO$_{x}$, N$_{2}$O), following \cite{olmer2017greenhouse}. These factors are each averaged from across the relevant pollutant factors associated with SSD, MSD, and HSD engine types, which together represent approximately 98\% of vessels engines \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. To get total emissions for each AIS message (i.e., emissions from the previous ping in a segment to the current one), we sum emissions from both engines and boilers. For VOCs, PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$, which do not have as detailed information on emissions factors, we instead multiply the CO$_{2}$ emissions by pollutant-specific conversion factors \cite{mayesshipping}. We also apply main engine low-load correction factors since engines operating at loads below 20\% operate inefficiently and emit more of certain pollutants based on how low the engine is operating (CH$_{4}$, CO, N$_{2}$O, NO$_{x}$, VOCs, PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$). We apply these correction factor on the main engine emissions for all AIS message with a main engine load below 20\%.

Starting January 1, 2020, the IMO mandated a low sulfur fuel switch, reducing the allowable sulfur content from 3.5\% to 0.5\% \cite{imo_sulphur_2020}. To account for this, for data starting on January 1, 2020 we apply a correction factor to SO$_{x}$, as well as PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$ which are partially produced by sulfur. The SO$_{x}$ emissions factor scales linearly with fuel sulfur percentage content \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. For pre-2020 data, we use an emissions factor based on a global average sulfur content of 2.5\% \cite{olmer2017greenhouse}. Assuming the sulfur content drops from 2.5\% to 0.5\% starting in 2020, this would correspond to an 80\% drop in sulfur content and an 80\% drop in the sulfur emissions factor. This 80\% is consistent with several studies that looked the the impact this new requirement had on global sulfur emissions (\cite{yuan2024abrupt, @yoshioka2024warming}). PM emissions factors are based on an equation that is non-linear to sulfur fuel content \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. Using this equation and again assuming an 80\% drop in sulfur content we estimate a 79.6\% drop in the PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$ emissions factors. We apply these adjusted emissions factors for all SO$_{x}$, PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$ emissions starting in January 1, 2020.


\subsubsection{Validating AIS-broadcasting emissions model}

We use two distinct emissions datasets for validation, one to assess the impact of updated vessel metadata from our random forest models on performance, and another to evaluate the model’s overall performance using emissions associated with actual vessel activity.

\paragraph{Registered data validation}

We have access to a confidential registered dataset of vessels' main engine fuel consumption measured during sea trials over a 24-hour period at a given speed, along with each vessel’s main engine power and design speed. Because these consumption values are not associated with specific trips that can be matched to our trip-level emissions estimates, direct comparison of derived emissions is not possible. However, this dataset allows us to validate the emissions generated by the main engine model module and assess the impact of updated metadata from our random forest models. Specifically, we compare the performance of the main engine model emissions estimates using main engine power and design speed values from the original IMO lookup table \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}  compared with those obtained when using our vessel-specific random forest model predictions of main engine power and design speed. Furthermore, we define the performance ceiling achievable through improved vessel metadata by testing the model with the actual engine power and design speed recorded in the registered dataset.

For a validation sample of 15,679 vessels, we ran the main engine model to calculate main engine energy use, incorporating the weather correction factor for offshore navigation along with the other default hull-fouling and draft correction factors from our main model, over a 24-hour period at the consumption speed provided in the registered dataset. We test three different sets of vessel metadata. For each set, main engine power and design speed were taken from either: (1) Table 81 lookup values from \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}; (2) our vessel-specific random forest model predictions; or (3) the registered dataset. Main engine energy use was then converted to emissions using the same CO$_{2}$ emission factor as in our main model. Since the registered dataset provides vessel-specific main fuel consumption values and not actual emissions values, we converted the registered fuel consumption to emissions by applying a fuel-to-CO$_{2}$ conversion factor of 3.12 (kg CO$_{2}$/tonne fuel), derived from the heavy fuel oil (HFO) and marine diesel oil (MDO) factors weighted by their contribution to global fuel consumption, as reported in \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}. This finally allows us to assess the performance of our model-estimated CO$_{2}$ emissions to those from the registered dataset.

This comparison shows that the updated vessel metadata using random forest models substantially improves model performance compared to that achieved using the original \cite{fourth2020greenhouse} vessel metadata, bringing the results closer to the model performance ceiling.

\paragraph{EU MRV data validation}

We also validate our CO$_{2}$ emissions model results against public emissions data from the European Maritime Safety Agency’s Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) program  \cite{mrv_emissions}. The MRV dataset provides yearly aggregated emissions—including CO$_{2}$ from main engines, auxiliary engines, gas turbines, boilers, and inert gas generators—along with hours at sea, and distance traveled for each vessel, covering voyages within, from, or to European Economic Area (EEA) “ports of call,” during which cargo is loaded/unloaded or passengers embark/disembark. It excludes port calls for refueling, supplies, crew changes, and similar activities.

Our AIS-derived emissions dataset does not contain detailed information on vessel activity while in port (i.e., whether or not cargo is loaded/unloaded or passengers embark/disembark), making it difficult to determine the exact same trip start and end points as defined in the MRV data, as well as which trips are included in the MRV records for a given year and vessel. To align vessel yearly activity across datasets, we: (1) filter our trip-level emissions data to include only trips that begin and/or end in EEA countries; (2) aggregate these trip-level emissions to annual emissions for each vessel; (3) apply varying matching tolerance margins to the MRV-reported annual hours at sea and select vessels-year from our dataset within these limits, in order to ensure that the trips we use for comparison have a total hours at sea roughly equal to that reported in the MRV dataset; and (4) compare annual MRV emissions with the annual sum of our trip-level emissions for the same vessel and year to evaluate model performance.

\subsection{Non-broadcasting vessels}

\subsubsection{Sentinel-1 SAR data description}

For our non-broadcasting vessel emission estimates, we leverage vessel detections from Sentinel-1 (S1) Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) imagery. The GFW dataset provides spatiotemporal information on each detection, including inferred vessel length and the likelihood of being classified as either a fishing vessel or non-fishing vessel. By identifying AIS-broadcasting vessels that are spatiotemporally matched to S1 detections, the dataset also categorizes each detection as either an AIS-broadcasting vessel ('matched') or a non-broadcasting vessel ('unmatched') \cite{kroodsma2022revealing,paolo2024satellite}. Additionally, detections are filtered to exclude repeated detections of vessels in anchorages, offshore infrastructure, potential ice, and other other ambiguities. Detections within a 1-km buffer from the shoreline are also excluded to reduce false positives from land-based structures and uncertainties arising from inaccurately defined, complex, and dynamic coastlines \cite{paolo2024satellite}. As a result, we used a dataset containing 29.3 million vessel detections from 2017 to 2024, of which 13.5 million detections were not matched to a broadcasting vessel ('unmatched'). These detections come from across more than 845,000 individual S1 scenes.

\subsubsection{Overview of non-broadcasting vessel emissions modeling framework}

We estimate emissions from non-broadcasting vessels using a three-model machine learning framework that uses a combination of AIS-derived emissions, S1 vessel detections, and additional spatiotemporal model features. For the first model, we build nine regression random forests (one for each of our three GHGs and six pollutants) to predict AIS-broadcasting emissions using matched S1 detections and additional sptatiotemporal model features (subsequently referred to as the 'emissions regression' model). We can then use this model to predict non-broadcasting emissions using unmatched S1 detections (i.e., detections from non-broadcasting vessels). For pixel-month observations that are imaged by S1, we can use the directly observed unmatched S1 detections for this prediction. For pixel-month observations that are not imaged by S1 (either because there were simply no S1 overpasses in this area and month, or because the area is completely outside the S1 footprint), we predict unmatched detections using a two-stage hurdle framework which is composed of two models: (1) a classification random forest that predicts the presence or absence of unmatched detections (subsequently referred to as the 'detection classification' model); and (2) a regression random forest regression to estimate the density of unmatched detections, conditional on there being any detections present  (subsequently referred to as the 'detection regression' model). Therefore, for pixel-months not imaged by S1, we can use the predicted density of unmatched detections from the 'detection regression' model as an input to the 'emissions regression' model in order to predict the amount of non-broadcasting emissions in these areas.

In the sections below, we detail: 1) the unit of observation and outcome variable for each of the three models; 2) the features used in each of the three models; 3) the data pre-processing steps used for each model; 4) the performance testing procedures and results for each model; 5) the feature importance results for each model; and 6) the final prediction procedure for obtaining our global spatiotemporal non-broadcasting vessel emissions estimates.

\subsubsection{Units of observation and outcome variables}

\paragraph{Emissions regression} We built nine regression random Forest models, for each of the three GHGs and six pollutants,  to predict emissions of each GHG and pollutant.  The observation unit is the total emissions in a given 1x1 degree ocean pixel, in a given month, for a given type of vessel (either non-fishing or fishing).  Because emissions are highly right-skewed, we log-normalize the emissions as the dependent variable. For model training, we used the emissions estimated from the AIS-broadcasting model, extracting  ping-level emissions from broadcasting vessels that occurred in ocean water beyond a 1 km buffer from shore, which corresponds to the spatial coverage of S1 detections processed by GFW and therefore excludes inland waterways, lakes, rivers, and ports. These ping-level emissions were aggregate to our unit of observation (by 1x1 degree pixel, month, and fishing/non-fishing vessel types), and observations with zero AIS-broadcasting emissions were excluded. Emissions for each observation are aggregated across all vessel sizes. To capture the vessel length distribution of S1 detections across different vessel sizes, for each observation we include 10 model features that represent the number of S1 detections in each vessel length decile.

\paragraph{Detection classification}  Each observation unit is the boolean presence/absence of unmatched S1 detections in a given 1x1 degree ocean pixel, in a given month, for a given type of vessel (either non-fishing or fishing), for a given length size bin decile.

\paragraph{Detection regression}  Each observation unit is the detection density of unmatched S1 detections in a given 1x1 degree ocean pixel, in a given month, for a given type of vessel (either non-fishing or fishing), for a given length size bin decile. To calculate the detection density, we normalize detections by the total km² imaged across all S1 scenes within each pixel and month to control for variation in Sentinel-1 sampling effort.

\subsubsection{Model features}

We define a global spatial resolution of 1x1-degree which is used for all spatial model features, and use a monthly temporal units for all temporal model features. All models features used across the three models are summarized in Table \ref{tab:data_sources} and described below.

\paragraph{S1 detection features}

S1 imagery enables identification and quantification of detections of both broadcasting vessels (i.e., those detections that can be matched to a vessel broadcasting AIS) and non-broadcasting vessels (i.e., those detections that are unmatched to a vessel broadcasting AIS) at specific locations and times. The vessel length of each detection is inferred and each detection is also classified as likely being a fishing vessel or non-fishing vessel\cite{paolo2024satellite}. We divide vessels into 10 size classes according to length deciles derived from all unmatched S1 detections that occurred from 2017 to 2024. These length-based decile thresholds are consistently applied to both AIS-derived vessel characteristics and Sentinel-1 detected vessel lengths in order to bin all AIS-broadcasting vessels and all unmatched S1 detections into length bins. Detections smaller than the minimum vessel size represented in the AIS-broadcasting dataset are excluded to avoid assigning emissions to vessel sizes not characterized by the AIS emissions model. We use length bins because inferred length is available for all S1 detections, and length is representative of main engine power (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-ais-length-power-relationship}), which is directly related to emissions in our bottom-up engineering model. We additionally stratify by vessel type (fishing or non-fishing) because some fishing vessel types have fundamentally different emissions profiles than non-fishing vessels (i.e., trawlers and dredgers emit more at low speeds due to the nature of their energy-intensive gear deployment, which is reflected in their gear-specific main engine load factor adjustment.). Additionally, to ensure emissions are correctly assigned from our AIS-based model, any matched detection that cannot be associated with a vessel in the AIS model is treated as a non-broadcasting vessel and classified as an unmatched detection. This prevents assigning emissions to vessels for which no emission estimates are available. We also normalize detections by the total km² imaged across all S1 scenes within each pixel and month to control for variation in Sentinel-1 sampling effort.

\paragraph{Spatial features}

For each pixel we extract spatial information, including depth, distance to shore, and distance to the nearest port, from a high-resolution GFW subproduct (0.01x0.01-degree) and aggregate it to our 1x1-degree grid resolution by computing the minimum value in each pixel. In addition to the distance from the nearest port (which can be of any size port), we also include the distance from each pixel centroid to the nearest of the top 20 container ports according to the Lloyd's list of 2024 \cite{LloydsListTopPorts2024}. We include this since traffic density, loitering practices, and navigation behavior in major ports differ from those in regular ports. Additional geographic features include the water area within each pixel (excluding land and the 1 km S1 buffer from shore) and a categorical variable indicating which of the five major ocean basins each pixel falls within.

\paragraph{Weather features}

Weather may influence both emissions and S1 detections. Sea conditions may affect not only vessel presence (e.g., smaller vessels may remain in port during storms), but also navigation behavior \cite{majamaki2025improving}. In addition, capillary waves generated by wind modify SAR background signal, directly affecting the ability of the S1 detection model to accurately identify vessels. To account for these effects, we extracted weather data from ECMWF Reanalysis v5 (ERA5), the fifth-generation atmospheric reanalysis of the global climate, produced by the Copernicus Climate Change Service \cite{era5cds2023}. We used Google Earth Engine to extract hourly ERA5 data and aggregate it to monthly minimum, maximum, and average values. Because ERA5 has higher spatial resolution than our 1×1-degree grid, we re-aggregated the data for each pixel, excluding land and the 1 km S1 buffer from shore, computing the mean of the monthly minimum, maximum, and average values, as well as the overall minimum and maximum across all sub-pixels (i.e., five values for each parameter). As our final model features, we used maximum wind speed (which influences both navigation conditions and S1 detection), average swell height (which affect navigation and vessel activity), and minimum swell period (which also affect navigation and vessel activity).

\paragraph{AIS reception quality features}

We included several model features related to AIS reception quality and signal type. To account for reception quality, we used two features: messages per day for AIS Type A and Type B transponders, capturing regional variation in AIS reception. Additionally, because AIS signals are received via different systems (terrestrial receivers, satellite receivers, and more recently dynamic receivers) we identified the associated receiver type for each ping and calculated the proportion of each type per pixel and month, summing to one. These features describing AIS coverage and the predominance of receiver types influence the fraction of broadcasting versus non-broadcasting vessels, and thus the associated emissions and detections. During data pre-processing, we reduce all features related to AIS reception quality into two components via principal component analysis (PCA) \cite{jolliffe2011principal}.

\paragraph{Sentinel satellite coverage features}

The temporal and spatial variability of S1 coverage can influence the estimation of emissions mainly derived through S1 detections. For each pixel and month in the emissions model, we therefore defined the average scene area by dividing the total pixel area imaged by the number of scenes, and calculated the ratio of this average scene area to the water area in the pixel. As a complementary measure of vessel presence at sea, we also incorporated Sentinel-2 (S2) detections. While S2 imagery was processed similarly to S1, providing aggregated fishing and non-fishing detections per km², we did not stratify S2 detections by length bins or fit a separate hurdle model for missing detections. Instead, we included an aggregate value of total S2 detections per km², along with the average scene area and area ratio (as for S1), to help inform vessel presence. We did not include S2 in the same way as S1 due to (1) less consistent temporal and spatial coverage of S2, which is strongly affected by cloud conditions, whereas S1 coverage is more constant throughout the year (i.e., S1 can see through clouds), and (2) greater confidence in the currently-available S1 detection products from GFW \cite{paolo2024satellite}. For the S1 detection hurdle model, the average scene areas and area ratios for S1 were calculated across the entire time series rather than monthly, to provide contextual information about historic satellite coverage. The hurdle model also included the total number of S1 scenes and total S1 pixel area imaged, while excluding S2 coverage features, and only considering unmatched S2 detections per km² imaged. During data pre-processing, we reduce all features related to sentinel satellite coverage into two components via PCA \cite{jolliffe2011principal}.

\paragraph{Temporal features}

We included year, month and day since start variables in all three models to account for temporal variability.

\subsubsection{Data pre-processing}

We perform the following data pre-processing steps, in this order, each time a model is trained or predictions are inferred. In order to avoid data leakage during performance testing and cross-validation, data pre-processing that performs calculations that directly use the data (i.e., imputation of missing values or PCA reduction) is always performed \textit{only} on the training data. To do so, we leverage the recipes package in R \cite{recipes}.

1. For each of latitude, longitude, and days-since-start, we use a natural spline transformation with 3 knots to create 3 new features that represent basis expansions of the original model feature, allowing us to capture non-linear affects of these spatiotemporal features.

2. We remove any numeric model features that have near-zero variance

3. We remove any numeric model features that are highly correlated with other features (i.e., that have a correlation of >0.9).

4. For any missing numeric model features, we impute their missing value over space and time using a linear model that is trained using the natural spline features for latitude, longitude, and days-since-start that were calculated in step 1.

5. For all features related to AIS reception quality, we reduce the features to a set of two principal components using PCA with centering and scaling \cite{jolliffe2011principal}.

6. For all features related to sentinel satellite coverage, we reduce the features to a set of two principal components using PCA with centering and scaling \cite{jolliffe2011principal}.

7. For the detection classification and regression models that are disaggregated by vessel length decile, we treat the length decile as a categorical model feature.

8. To handle class imbalance in the detection classification model, when fitting the detection classiciation model we downsample the majority-class observations to match the number of minority-class observations. We apply this downsampling only ever to the training data, and always test using the full testing dataset (this occurred during both the overall model performance testing, as well as for each fold during cross-validation).

9. Due to the large size of the detections classification training dataset (more than 16 million rows of data), when fitting this model we additionally randomly subsample the training dataset to only use 25\% of the observations during training. Again, we always still use the entire testing dataset for testing.

\subsubsection{Model training}

We defined three independent Random Forest models using the tidymodels framework in R \cite{tidymodels}. Model fitting was done using the ranger engine \cite{ranger} in parsnip \cite{parsnip}. with 500 trees. We tuned the ranger *mtry* and *min\_n* hyperparameters using a space-filling grid of size 10, and 5-fold cross-validation. For the emission regression and detection regression models, we optimized the hyperparameters to maximize traditional R$^{2}$. For the detection classification model, we optimized the hyperparameters to maximize the area under the Precision-Recall curve in order to find a model that maximizes class separation. We  then further optimized the detection presence/absence classification cutoff threshold to maximize the F1 score (the harmonic mean of precision and recall). 

\subsubsection{Model testing}

We assessed model performance for each of the three models using two different evaluation approaches designed to test: 1) predictive power for areas within the S1 footprint but that don't have overpasses in a given month (subsequently referred to as 'inside footprint'); and 2) and generalization to locations completely outside the S1 footprint subsequently referred to as 'outside footprint').

\paragraph{Inside footprint testing}

Because both emissions and detection predictions inside the S1 footprint are made for locations where emissions and detections data is available for training, (i.e., inside the footprint we are not generalizing to regions with unknown emissions and detections), we first evaluated performance inside the footprint using a stratified random 75/25 train/test split. For each of the three models, we stratify the random split by the dependent variable (presence/absence of unmatched detections for the detections classification model; density of unmatched detections for the detections regression model; and log-transformed emissions for the emissions regression model).

\paragraph{Outside footprint testing}

Then, to assess spatial transferability when predicting emissions in areas outside the footprint or near its edges that are poorly represented in the training data, we additionally implemented a spatially structured split, training on nearshore pixels and testing on offshore pixels. This nearshore/offshore split is constructed separately for each of the five major ocean basins by ranking pixels according to their minimum distance to port and designating the farthest 25\% as offshore, with the remaining 75\% used for training. Additionally, to simulate our ability to predict detections outside the S1 footprint, we manually set all sentinel-related model features in the testing split to missing values, since these values will not be available at the time of prediction for pixels outside the S1 footprint. This strategy tests the ability of the modeling framework to predict emissions in regions outside the S1 footprint, although such areas contribute only 1.1\% to the total non-broadcasting emissions (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-spatial-coverage-footprint}).

Classifier performance is reported using precision-recall curves (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-pr-curves}), receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-roc-curves}), and confusion matrices (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-conf-mat}). Table \ref{tab:all_performance_metrics_table} also summarizes regression performance for detections, both inside and outside the footprint, alongside the emissions model performance. To assess the performance of the emissions model, predicted log emissions were back-transformed using Duan’s smearing coefficient, calculated from the residuals of the training data \cite{duan1983smearing}. The same transformation was applied to the final model prediction.

\subsubsection{Feature importance}

After hyperparameter tuning on the full dataset, we performed a final fit using the best model and analyzed the feature importance for the three non-broadcasting emissions models (Fig \ref{fig:fig-feature-importance}). Feature importance was quantified using the bias-corrected Gini index \cite{nembrini2018revival}, as implemented for random forests in the ranger R package \cite{ranger}. This metric quantifies how much each model feature reduces node impurity when it is used to make splits in the random forest's decision trees. Intuitively, looking at emissions regression model, the importance of model features quantifying the number of matched S1 detections for each length bin are ordered according to the length bin size: features describing detections in larger bins (i.e., for larger vessels) are more important for predicting aggregate emissions than those describing detections in smaller bins (i.e., for smaller vessels).

\subsubsection{Predicting non-broadcasting emissions}

To generate the final global estimates of non-broadcasting vessel emissions, we applied the trained models across all ocean pixels on a monthly basis for the entire 2017-2024 study period. The prediction workflow followed a hierarchical logic based on the availability of S1 detections:

1. Direction estimation: If unmatched S1 detections are available for a given pixel-month, we use the unmatched detection density to directly predict non-broadcasting emissions using the emissions regression model.
2. Indirect estimation: If unmatched S1 detections are not available for a given pixel-month, we: a) predict whether or not unmatched detections are present using the classification first stage of the hurdle model; then b) if unmatched detections are predicted to be present, we predict the density of unmatched detections using the regression second stage of the hurdle model; and then finally c) we use the predicted density of unmatched detections in the emissions regression model to predict emissions. 

The final output provides a continuous global dataset of monthly non-broadcasting emissions from 2017 to 2024 at 1×1-degree resolution, disaggregated by fishing and non-fishing vessel types.

\paragraph{Direct estimation}

For this prediction, we replaced the 10 matched S1 detection density features used during model training with the observed unmatched S1 detection density for each of the 10 length bin features and separately for non-fishing/fishing vessel types. Additionally, we also replaced the total S2 matched detections model feature with total S2 unmatched detections, which is subsequently used for the sentinel-related PCA features.  We were able to use this direct estimation approach for 86.4\% of the total estimated non-broadcasting emissions (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-spatial-coverage-footprint}). This approach predicts non-broadcasting emissions fully within the S1 footprint, and thus also represents the models with the highest predictive performance (Table \ref{tab:all_performance_metrics_table}).

\paragraph{Indirect estimation}

For pixel-months where S1 detections were unavailable, either because they were not imaged due to spatiotemporal satellite heterogeneity or because they fall fully outside the S1 footprint, we first used the detections classification model to predict whether detections were present or absent for each of the 10 length bins and fishing/non-fishing vessel types. If detections were predicted, the subsequent detection regression model was used to predict the number of unmatched S1 detections per km² for each length bin and fishing/non-fishing vessel type. These predicted unmatched detection densities were then fed into the emissions regression model as the 10 separate length-bin-related model features (for each fishing/non-fishing vessel type) to predict the resulting emissions. We do this for all nine emissions regression models corresponding to the three GHGs and six  pollutants. Of the total predicted non-broadcasting emissions, 12.5\% corresponded to indirect estimations within the S1 footprint, while only 1.1\% occurred entirely outside the footprint (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-spatial-coverage-footprint}).

\subsection{Comparing our emission estimates to other inventories}

Our total emissions estimates (summed across broadcasting and non-broadcasting vessels) are consistent with other well-known global inventories: the International Maritime Organization (IMO) \cite{fourth2020greenhouse}, the EU's Emissions Database for Global Atmospheric Research (EDGAR) \cite{JRC143227}, and the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) experimental shipping database \cite{clarke2023co2} (Fig. \ref{fig:fig-inventory-comparison} and Table \ref{tab:inventory_comparison}). For each inventory, we pull all available years of data from 2017 through 2024. Both the IMO and OECD inventories leverage AIS data, with IMO using a bottom-up engineering model similar to ours, and OECD leveraging a machine learning model trained on the EU MRV database that uses AIS movement data to inform certain model features and which uses this model to make predictions for AIS-broadcasting vessels not contained in the EU MRV database. The EDGAR inventory uses a top-down approach that leverages regional fuel use statistics. Our approach combining AIS and S1 data yields the highest estimates, as expected. The level of general agreement for this complex estimate across multiple sources provides support for the reliability and utility of our methodology and results.

\subsection{Disentangling trends in AIS-based emissions data: the evolution of AIS data providers over time}

\subsection{Disentangling trends in AIS-based emissions data: the introduction of Dynamic AIS}

AIS messages are broadcast from vessels and then received through one of three technologies: satellite-based receivers, terrestrial receivers, and dynamic receivers. Satellite and terrestrial receivers are the oldest receiver types, while 2022 saw the widespread adoption of 'Dynamic-AIS' in several regions throughout the world. Dynamic AIS is a technology that is implemented by Spire, one of the major AIS data providers, in which satellite-enabled AIS receivers are installed on vessels that travel through some of the world's busiest shipping lanes  such as the South China Sea \cite{spire_documentation_2022}. Through GFW, we can determine the receiver type for every single AIS message in the dataset over time. In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}, we summarize the amount of estimated emissions and the number of distinct vessels that were detected by the three different types of AIS receivers. In 2022, we can clearly see a sharp rise in the amount of emissions that were estimated from Dynamic AIS, which intuitively coincides with a decrease in the emissions that are estimated from satellite and terrestrial receivers. On aggregate, this led to an increase in total estimated emissions. The number of distinct vessels detected by dynamic AIS also sharply rose in 2022, almost equaling the number of vessels detected by all receiver types.

\end{figure}

In Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type-top-flags}, we see the amount of quantified emissions for the top 10 vessel flags broken apart by AIS receiver type. The trends are similar to those seen on aggregate ( Fig. \ref{fig:fig-annual-emissions-by-ais-receiver-type}), although there is variation in terms of the increase in Dynamic AIS detections across different flags. This is likely driven by whether or not each flag travels through regions where Dynamic AIS was widely adopted. It is important to note that towards the end of 2021, China experience a blackout of terrestrial AIS signals due to new privacy laws that were being implemented \cite{benefits_dybnamic_ais}. This sharp decline from 2021 to 2022 in terrestrial-based emissions is clearly evident for China. Without the introduction of Dynamic AIS, it is possible that this disruption may have resulted in erroneously small emissions in China following the terrestrial receiver blackout. This is another example of how multiple complementary technologies can be fused in order to maximize emissions coverage over time.
